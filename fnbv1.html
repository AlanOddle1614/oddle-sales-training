Module 1 · Understanding F&B

Understand the industry before you ever pitch the product.

Restaurant owners respect reps who understand their business. This module turns you into that rep — fluent in the numbers operators live with, the gaps they quietly bleed from, and the archetypes they fall into. Nothing here sells Oddle. Everything here earns you the right to.
~ 90 min read No pre-requisites

01 · The Three Goals

Strip away the lease complaint, the staffing drama, the bad review, the viral post — every F&B operator, everywhere, is asking for the same three outcomes. They aren't independent. They compound.

Goal 01
Grow Revenue
Acquire new customers. Bring them back more often. Lift the cheque when they return. Fixed costs — rent, labour, utilities — don't move when a few hundred more covers walk in. So most of every new dollar drops to profit. Revenue growth is the single biggest lever on the P&L.
Goal 02
Control Cost
Stop the bleed. Marketplace commissions. Overlapping SaaS. Tools paid for but never used. Marketing spend that can't be measured. On a 3–5% net-margin business, a dollar saved is worth twenty earned. For the operator, cost discipline IS revenue growth.
Goal 03
Know the Customer
Who they are. Where they live. How often they come. What brought them in. What stopped them coming back. Without this, every marketing, menu, and pricing decision is a guess. Data isn't a fourth goal — it's what makes the other two possible.
The universal rule. You cannot grow revenue or control cost at scale without knowing the customer. Every Oddle product exists to move one of these three forward. If a rep can't tie their pitch back to one of these three in a single sentence — they haven't earned the right to pitch.

02 · Industry Context

F&B is a thin-margin business under compounding pressure. Before we talk about solving the operator's problem, understand the shape of the problem.

A. Thin-margin business

Most of a restaurant's dollar is already spoken for before the owner sees it. The four big buckets of cost:

Cost of Goods
20 – 35%
Labour
20 – 30%
Rental
20 – 25%
Overheads / Other
5 – 10%

What's left over after all four — that's net margin. And the spread is brutal:

Average
3 – 5%
Most restaurants
Successful
10 – 15%
Well-run operations
High-margin
20 – 30%
Rare — brand + scale
What this means for the rep. Assume the operator across the table is running a 3–5% net-margin business until proven otherwise. Every dollar of cost savings is worth ~20 dollars of new revenue to them. Every dollar of new revenue that doesn't bring new variable cost drops almost entirely to profit. Know which lever you're pulling.

B. Tech laggard industry

F&B is one of the slowest-adopting industries for technology. Two patterns repeat:

  • Most operators are either not adopting tech at all, or adopting late and reluctantly.
  • Even after they adopt, they typically don't capitalise on it for growth — they use tech operationally, not strategically.

The core problem: operators don't know how to use tech effectively. They buy tools, plug them in, and move on. No one teaches them what lever each tool pulls. This is the single biggest opening for a consultative rep.

C. ~85% of first-time customers don't return within a year

Across a sample of 200 F&B brands we work with, the first-year return rate is stark — and it varies sharply by segment. The 85% average hides a much wider distribution underneath.

The takeaway. The average restaurant acquires a customer, feeds them once, and never sees them again. That's not a marketing problem — it's a data and engagement problem. They can't re-engage people they can't identify.

D. ~75% of dine-in customers cannot be identified

Even the restaurants that think they "know" their customers — don't. Two realities operators underestimate:

Reservation math. Assume a fully booked restaurant. Average covers per reservation ≈ 4. Only the person who booked is captured — name, phone, email. The other 3 of every 4 diners walk out unidentified. That's 75% invisible at best. And most restaurants aren't 100% reservation — the walk-ins are 100% invisible. So on average, captured data is well below 25%.
Loyalty programs don't fix it. In most loyalty programs, only the person who pays is identified. Their dining companions stay invisible. Add to that: a share of customers never join the program to begin with. You're capturing a fraction of a fraction.

This is the data gap — and it's what makes every other growth lever harder.

E. The 5 core challenges every operator is navigating

1
Increasing competitionRetail is dying. That floorspace is being taken up by F&B. More options for consumers, but the number of consumers stays the same.
2
Increasing costFood inputs, rent, energy, packaging — all compounding. Price elasticity on the menu is weak.
3
Manpower shortageF&B hiring is structurally short across SG, MY, TW. Wages rise while productivity doesn't.
4
Evolving consumer behaviourDelivery, dine-in, pickup, group orders, pre-orders — consumers expect all channels, on their schedule.
5
More digital channels to managePOS, reservation, delivery platforms, marketing, socials, review sites — each needs attention, each pays someone a fee.

03 · Restaurant Economics

The single biggest moat between a pitcher and an educator is this: can you read a restaurant's P&L? A rep who can explain why a small revenue lift drops disproportionately to the bottom line wins the conversation before they've pitched a product.

A simplified P&L — the average restaurant

This is the shape of the business. Midpoints taken from the ranges above. It lands on a realistic 5% net margin — the ceiling for the average restaurant.

Line itemAmount% of revenue
Revenue$100,000100%
Cost of Goods($28,000)28%
Labour($25,000)25%
Rental($22,000)22%
Overheads (utilities, admin, supplies)($7,500)7.5%
Other operating (marketing, commissions, SaaS)($12,500)12.5%
Net Profit$5,0005%

Why a 5% revenue lift produces a disproportionate profit lift

Labour, rental, and most overheads are fixed — they don't scale with a few hundred more covers. Only Cost of Goods scales with revenue. So when new revenue comes in that doesn't bring new labour or new rent, almost all of it falls to the bottom line.

Line itemBaselineAfter 5% liftΔ
Revenue$100,000$105,000+$5,000
Cost of Goods (scales)($28,000)($29,400)($1,400)
All other costs (fixed)($67,000)($67,000)—
Net Profit$5,000$8,600+$3,600
Profit lift——+72%
The math every rep must carry. A 5% revenue lift from existing operating capacity produces a 50–70% profit lift. Why? Because only ~28 cents of every new dollar becomes cost. The other ~72 cents is pure profit. This is the single most important number to anchor any revenue-growth pitch.

04 · The Six Gaps

Every restaurant you meet will have some combination of these six gaps. Your job isn't to pitch products — it's to diagnose which gaps they have, surface them through questions, and let the operator self-diagnose. Only then is the door open.

Gap 1 · Data

Data Gap

The restaurant captures almost no customer data. Reservations give you one name per group. POS gives you an order, not a person. Dine-in walk-ins leave as strangers. The industry baseline is a 25% capture rate — 75 of every 100 diners are invisible.

"How many diners do you serve on a monthly basis? How many reservation bookings do you get per month? The difference is the data gap."
Feeds: Revenue Leakage · Product home: Enrolment, CDP
Gap 2 · Insight

Insight Gap

Two sub-problems. Siloed data — POS, reservations, delivery, enrolment all sit in separate systems and don't talk to each other. And missing depth — no customer proximity, no acquisition source, no reason-for-lapse. The data you need to make decisions doesn't exist.

"If I asked you which daypart has your highest repeat rate, or what percentage of your customers live within 3km — could you answer?"
Feeds: Revenue Leakage · Product home: CDP, Enrolment
Gap 3 · Engagement

Engagement Gap

No mechanism to bring customers back. Either (a) no data, so no outreach possible, or (b) the data exists but is sitting idle — no campaigns, no automation, no reason for the customer to come back.

"When was the last time you sent a message to 1,000+ customers with something genuinely useful to them?"
Feeds: Revenue Leakage · Product home: Email Marketing, Rewards
Gap 4 · Revenue

Revenue Gap

Three sub-problems. No organic online channel — corporate and group orders go nowhere, or to marketplaces at 30% commission. No retention mechanism — repeat rates stagnate at 8–15%. No playbook — tools sit idle without a strategy to grow with them.

"If a corporate wanted to place a $200 lunch order for their team today, where would they go?"
Feeds: Revenue Leakage · Product home: eShop, Reserve, Email Marketing
Gap 5 · Cost

Cost Gap

Margin bleeding in three ways: commissions to marketplaces (25–35% per order), a fragmented tool stack paying multiple vendors for overlapping capabilities, and features paid for that are never used.

"Based on how much you pay for your reservation system and the number of bookings you get, what does each online booking cost you?"
"How much are you paying for your email marketing tool plus reservation system plus eShop system — combined?"
Feeds: Cost Leakage · Product home: eShop, All Access Plan
Gap 6 · Execution

Execution Gap

The operator has the tool (eShop is live) — but doesn't know which lever to pull to actually grow online sales. And the vendor who sold the system offers no support to help them. Tools live, growth doesn't follow.

"If you want to improve your online sales, do you know which lever to pull?"
Revenue = Traffic × Conversion × ABS
"Do you know what impacts conversion?"
Why this is a gap: most operators can't answer, which IS the gap. Most of their vendors can't either. Oddle's role is to help them pull each lever.
Feeds: Revenue & Cost Leakage · Product home: Full stack + Oddle execution support

Before the 8 archetypes — here's how we derived them. A rep needs to know the logic, not just the labels, so they can classify any restaurant confidently in under a minute.

Why we didn't build a 400-cell matrix

4 × 5 × 4 × 5 = ~400 theoretical combinations. After pruning invalid combos (Cloud Kitchen is always delivery-only, Fine Dining rarely QSR-shaped, etc.), ~40–60 real combos remain. Still too many for a rep to hold in their head.

How we landed on 8

  • Establishment Maturity × Format — archetype-defining. This is what the restaurant is.
  • Channel Mix + eShop Stance — diagnostic overlays. This is what the restaurant has right now, captured during discovery.

95% of real prospects pattern-match to one of 8 named archetypes. The overlays sharpen the motion once you've classified.

07 · The Eight Archetypes

For each archetype: the profile, the gaps you'll find, the likely objection, and the recommended sales motion. Learn these and you can calibrate your approach before the first question is asked.

Heritage Independent
Traditional × Casual Full Service / Eatery

Family-run heritage restaurants and eateries — 10+ years old, deep brand equity, deeply loyal customers, limited digital adoption. Not hawkers, not QSR chains — though the rare very-popular hawker stall can fit here (they'll still never take Reserve).

Typical gaps

Reserve — only if they accept reservations. They're almost certainly doing manual bookings today (Data + Execution Gap). But many heritage operators are too old-school to adopt booking tech. Gauge first; don't force it.
Enrolment — in principle, yes. They have almost no data. In practice, this is one step too far for most heritage operators unless a younger generation has taken over the business.

New × Any format

08 · Tech Stack Check

Always check the tech stack of a restaurant in discovery. The tech stack is how you turn archetype into motion. But not every element carries equal weight — online ordering is the one signal that dictates the entire pitch. The rest are diagnostic inputs.

Online ordering — the signal that sets the entire pitch

Reservation choice is a feature / cost debate. POS is a budget decision. Enrolment — almost nobody has anything real. But online ordering is different. What you see on eShop posture tells you which of three conversations you're about to have. It's a worldview signal — it reveals whether the operator sees customers as theirs to own or theirs to rent — and that single read reshapes the motion.

Scenario 1Longest conversation — moving belief
No marketplace. No eShop. Not selling online at all.
What you see Dine-in or takeaway only. No delivery presence anywhere. Either the operator doesn't believe the demand is there, or they're scared of the ops complexity.
What it means You're not swapping a vendor — you're moving belief on the category itself. The sell isn't Oddle yet. It's the case for having any online channel at all.
The pitch Show them the organic demand they're leaving on the table — delivery, corporate lunch orders, group pre-orders, festive bulk (mooncake, CNY, Christmas). Then prove Oddle can operationalise it without burning their floor team (order cutoffs, kitchen batching, delivery partner flow).
Scenario 2Medium conversation — selling the second channel
On delivery marketplaces only (GrabFood, Foodpanda, Deliveroo, Uber Eats). No direct eShop.
What you see Delivery revenue is flowing — but through rented channels. Every order costs 25–30% commission. No customer data. No corporate / group / bulk order capture. No way to reach past customers directly.
What it means They believe in delivery. What they haven't seen yet is the value of owning the channel. Your job is to close the second gap — not sell delivery, sell ownership.
The pitch Use the ownership analogy — "Nike has its own store on Nike.com alongside Zalora. McDonald's runs McDelivery alongside GrabFood. Why are you letting GrabFood be your only delivery channel?" Marketplaces rent you traffic at a tax. An organic eShop captures corporate orders, group pre-orders, and repeat customers at 0% commission — and you finally own the customer data to market back to them.
Scenario 3Shortest conversation — diagnostic, not persuasion
Has organic eShop (on Oddle, a competitor, or hybrid with marketplace).
What you see They already own a direct channel. Belief in the category is not the gap. The question is whether that channel is actually performing.
What it means Don't re-sell the category — you'll lose them in 30 seconds if you do. Switch into diagnostic mode. Run their current setup against the 6 gaps.
The pitch The two gaps that bite hardest here: Cost Gap — what are they paying? Commission % · platform fees · plug-in costs · payment gateway markup. And Execution Gap — run Traffic × Conversion × ABS against their actual numbers. Is traffic arriving and not converting? Is the basket small? Is the shop a parked website? Then position Oddle as the upgrade (cleaner ops, better conversion, integrated CDP, no plug-in tax) — not as the first online channel.
Edge cases.  Hybrid (marketplace + organic eShop) → treat as Scenario 3 for the diagnostic; secondary move is shifting order share from marketplace to organic once they see the margin difference.  N/A (fine dining, some heritage dine-in-only) → eShop as a category may not apply, but festive pre-orders (mooncake / CNY / Christmas hampers) usually do — probe there instead.
Why online ordering uniquely matters. Reservation vendor, POS, enrolment tool — these are mostly budget, feature, or UI preferences. The operator's answer there tells you what to sell against, but the pitch structure doesn't change. eShop posture is different: it's a worldview decision that dictates whether you're moving belief (Scenario 1), selling ownership (Scenario 2), or diagnosing performance (Scenario 3). One question — three entirely different calls.

Reservation stack — a signal, not a belief

If a merchant is running multiple reservation systems or leaning on aggregators (Inline, Quandoo, Chope, OpenTable, Eatigo), they're typically chasing more exposure and sales. What they often forget: ~90% of reservations are organic, not sourced from third-party aggregators. The aggregator fee is largely paying for bookings the restaurant would have received anyway.

Other tech stacks — diagnostic inputs

Enrolment, POS, marketing channels — each of these tells you something about gaps, but none deserve dedicated sections. Capture them, probe against the 6 gaps, move on.

  • Enrolment: None · Paper stamp · Plastic card · Digital (which)
  • POS: Standalone · POS + bundle (StoreHub / Eats365 / iChef) · Integrated with other tools
  • Marketing channel: Email (tool) · WhatsApp broadcast · Social (organic / paid) · SMS · None
