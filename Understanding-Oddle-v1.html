Understanding Oddle

Before the rep opens their mouth on a pitch, they must answer five questions in their head without hesitation: What is Oddle? Who is it for? What enemy are we fighting? What world are we promising? What's the integrated system that gets the restaurant there? This module locks those answers.

~45 min read · 2× review Pre-reads: Modules 1 & 2 Outcome: rep can deliver the Oddle story cold, in 90 seconds, no slides

Why this module exists

A rep who can't articulate Oddle in 90 seconds cannot sell Oddle. Every moment after that — the pitch, the demo, the close — degrades if the rep is unsure what they're representing. Confusion in the rep becomes objection in the merchant.

This module is the narrative spine — the enemy, the promised land, the integrated system that gets the restaurant there. Once a rep believes the story, the rest of the program just equips them to tell it well.

Rep story spine: enemy → promised land → integrated revenue system → proof → ask. Five beats, every time. Every line of this module is a piece of one of those beats.

Oddle in 30 seconds

If a rep is pinned down in a lift and has 30 seconds, this is what comes out:

Oddle is the restaurant growth platform that helps restaurants win new guests, understand who they are, and turn them into returning guests — all from one connected system.

Tagline: Be the restaurant guests choose first — from search, to booking, to their next visit.

Why this phrasing, not another: It names the job (win new → know who → make return), not the tools. It names the outcome (chosen first), not the feature list. It separates us from marketplaces ("your restaurant") and from point tools ("one connected system"). Every word is load-bearing — none should be swapped.

The 90-second version (for meetings)

When the rep has 90 seconds across the table, the spine expands to five beats:

  1. Name the enemy. "Most restaurants are running a mix of operational tech that helps them run, not grow — plus marketplaces that rent them customers they never own."
  2. Promise the land. "Imagine every channel — delivery, dine-in, reservations — feeding one view of your guest. And you know what to do with that guest tomorrow."
  3. Show the integrated system. "That's Oddle. One integrated revenue system. Six products, built only for restaurants."
  4. Prove it. "[Restaurant X] doubled their identified guests in six months and added [Y%] of revenue from repeat visits."
  5. Ask. "Worth 30 minutes on a diagnostic of your numbers?"

The origin story

Oddle wasn't built by tech people trying to sell software to restaurants. It was built by restaurant people who got tired of renting their growth engine.

The world Oddle rejected

Footlocker & GrabFood

Third-party retailers and aggregators. They sell for you — but you don't know who your customer is, they own the relationship, and they take 20–30% of every transaction forever.

  • You pay for every order, forever
  • You don't know who bought from you
  • They control your ranking, your pricing, your customer

The world Oddle chose

Nike.com & McDelivery

Own the channel, own the customer. Nike still sells through Footlocker — but Nike.com is where the brand relationship lives. McDonald's still appears on GrabFood — but McDelivery is where the loyalty, the data, and the repeat visits live.

  • Direct relationship with the guest
  • Data lives in your house, not someone else's
  • Repeat revenue compounds — doesn't leak

Twelve years ago Oddle launched with one product — Oddle Shop — so restaurants could run their own eShop, the Nike.com of food. Over the last decade we've added Reserve, Enrolment, Virtual Lounge, CDP + Email, and Terminal. Every product answers the same question: how does the restaurant own more of its own growth?

Why restaurants

Oddle is not a bet taken from the outside looking in. It is built by people who lived the pain of the industry — from both sides of the table. That is the credibility bar the rep leans on when a merchant asks "why should I trust you lot?"

The operator's side

Jonathan — Founder, Oddle

Jonathan is a restaurateur. He owns a restaurant and knows the pain of running one — the thin margins, the guest who never comes back, the marketplace commission eating into the month. Every product decision at Oddle traces back to one test: would this have helped me when I was the operator?

The demand side

Alan — CRO, Oddle

Alan came from HungryGoWhere — the F&B recommendation and reservation portal. He saw restaurant pain from the other side: how guests chose, how they churned, how marketplaces captured the customer the restaurant should have owned. The Oddle commercial motion is shaped by that view.

Passionate about the industry — we built something to serve it. Oddle exists because the people building it were already in restaurants, on both sides of the table, and couldn't find a platform that treated growth like the operator does. So we built it.

Why restaurants deserve their own platform

Founder conviction is the reason Oddle exists. The structural reason Oddle is F&B-only — and doesn't dilute into adjacent verticals — sits on three facts:

  1. Restaurants sell to humans, not accounts. The unit economics — repeat visits, party size, day-parts, seasonality — look nothing like SaaS buyer journeys. A platform built for generic SMBs can't price, capture, or segment the way F&B needs.
  2. Restaurants live and die by the guest relationship. Food is commodity; choosing your restaurant over the one next door is almost entirely about brand trust, familiarity, and memory. That is a data and engagement problem — not a POS problem.
  3. The F&B operator needs one partner, not eight vendors. The operator is already running a kitchen, managing staff, and thinking about next week's menu. They don't have time to stitch together a stack. One integrated system is not a luxury — it's the only way adoption survives the first 90 days.
How a rep uses this section in the field. When a merchant pushes back with "another tech vendor promising the world" — don't argue features. Lead with the founder credibility: "Our founder Jonathan runs a restaurant. Our CRO Alan came out of HungryGoWhere. This wasn't built for restaurants — it was built by restaurants." Then move to the three reasons above only if the merchant asks for more.

The enemy

Every story needs one villain. Ours is named carefully — because a sloppy villain creates bad objections.

The enemy is Operational Tech. Tools that help restaurants run, but not grow. They sit in silos. They don't talk to each other. Restaurants that lean on marketplaces to grow end up trapped — renting customers they never own.

Three traps collapse under the same label. The rep must be able to name all three and which one the merchant in front of them is sitting in.

Trap 1 · Silo
Tools that don't talk

POS, reservation system, loyalty app, email tool — each bought separately, each a separate login, none sharing a guest identity. Merchant has data in five places and insight in none.

Trap 2 · Rent
Marketplace dependence

Merchant uses Grab/Foodpanda/Chope to "grow." The orders arrive — but the guest belongs to the marketplace. The merchant pays 20–30% in commissions and cannot contact the guest to bring them back.

Trap 3 · Ops
Ops tools masquerading as growth

POS and inventory systems sold as "customer engagement." They help count what happened yesterday. They do not help win tomorrow's guest. Merchant confuses running the restaurant with growing it.

Rep discipline — never name the enemy as a specific company. The villain is the category, not a brand. Reps who trash Chope, Grab, or a named POS vendor sound small and lose credibility. "Siloed operational tech" is the villain. Specific competitors are handled separately in the "What Oddle is NOT" section below.

New → Known → Repeat — the methodology

Before any product, the methodology. Three jobs the restaurant needs done, in order. The rep owns this as the memory handle that every product then snaps into.

Stage 1
New

Win guests the restaurant didn't have yesterday. Get them through the door — physically or digitally — and capture the transaction.

Stage 2
Known

Turn every interaction into an identified guest record. Anonymous walk-ins become names, emails, phone numbers — the fuel for everything downstream.

Stage 3
Repeat

Bring the known guest back. Segment by behaviour, automate the right message at the right moment, measure the revenue that comes from returning visits.

The pitch memory handle: merchants who hear a list of six products get lost. Merchants who hear "three jobs — win them, know them, bring them back" remember it at dinner with their business partner that night. Spine first, products second.

The compounding loop

Each stage feeds the next. New fills the pipe. Known stores the name. Repeat converts the name into revenue — and that returning guest is a new New at a higher margin, because acquisition was paid once. A restaurant operating the full loop has net margin that pulls away from a restaurant running any one part in isolation.

Why Oddle is different

Merchants will compare us to generic SaaS, to marketplaces, to point tools, and to their existing POS. Four differentiators must be locked in the rep's head so the comparison always lands on us.

Differentiator 1
Restaurant-only

We build only for F&B. Every feature assumes party size, day-parts, walk-ins, cover turns, chef-driven menus. A generic SaaS serving "SMBs" cannot model these natively. Depth, not breadth, is the moat.

Differentiator 2
One ecosystem, one partner

Six products, one contract, one dashboard, one data layer, one account team. Competitors stitch together point tools; Oddle ships as a system. Integration is not a future roadmap item — it's the product.

Differentiator 3
Real intelligence, not reporting

Most "analytics" in F&B is a dashboard of what happened last week. Oddle's data layer is structured for next-action recommendations — who to target, what to send, when to send it — not retrospective PDFs.

Differentiator 4
Partner, not vendor

We measure ourselves on the restaurant's revenue growth, not on our license fee. Pricing is structured so that our upside aligns with the restaurant's. Skin in the game, not software-as-a-subscription.

If we want the claim to survive a sharp CFO, reps need a specific answer for "how exactly is Oddle's upside aligned with mine?". Need Alan's preferred 1-liner.

Category positioning — What Oddle is NOT

Four categories a merchant will confuse Oddle with. For each: the merchant's actual objection, and the rep's answer — memorised verbatim.

✕ Oddle is NOT a marketplace. vs. GrabFood / Foodpanda / Deliveroo
"I already use GrabFood. Why do I need another channel?"
GrabFood is a marketplace — you rent their customer base and pay 20–30% on every order, forever. You never know who that customer is. Oddle Shop is your eShop — you own the customer, you own the data, and you pay a far lower margin. You keep GrabFood for reach; you run Oddle for margin and for the relationship.
✕ Oddle is NOT a reservation vendor. vs. Chope / SevenRooms
"I already have Chope / SevenRooms. Why switch?"
Chope and SevenRooms solve bookings — we cover bookings and the entire lifecycle after: identification at the table, post-meal engagement, email automation, and repeat revenue. You can do everything you do today with Chope using Oddle, often for the same or less — with a clearer path to growth because all of it lives in one system instead of three.
✕ Oddle is NOT a POS with a loyalty add-on. vs. POS-bundled loyalty
"My POS already has loyalty built in — why do I need Oddle?"
A POS loyalty bolt-on counts points at the counter. It doesn't capture the dine-in guest at the table, doesn't run reservations, doesn't drive an eShop, doesn't segment or trigger email. It's a punch-card, not a growth engine. Oddle sits alongside your POS and does the work a POS was never designed to do — win new guests and bring them back.
✕ Oddle is NOT your own e-ordering site. vs. custom-built website / Shopify / WP plugin
"I already built my own ordering site — why do I need Oddle Shop?"
A DIY site takes orders. That's stage one of five. It doesn't integrate with your reservation flow, it doesn't identify walk-ins, it doesn't feed a CDP, and it doesn't run automated repeat-visit campaigns. You'll end up buying four more tools to finish the job — and paying engineers to stitch them together. Oddle is the whole system on day one.
Never-break framing in these rebuttals: Oddle is never positioned as "cheaper than X." Oddle is always positioned as "a different category of solution." Price compression is a vendor game. We are not vendors.

Never-break language rules

Small word choices carry outsized weight in the buyer's head. A rep who says "online ordering" instead of "eShop" has already given away ground. The rules below are non-negotiable.

Say: eShop — a NEW channel
Don't say: online ordering
"Online ordering" sounds like a utility. "eShop" frames it as a branded, owned channel — like Nike.com. It's the single word that separates us from Deliveroo / GrabFood category in the merchant's head.
Say: partner
Don't say: vendor / supplier
Vendors sell and disappear. Partners win together. Everything about how we price, onboard, and measure success is built to earn the partner frame. Don't give it away with one lazy word.
Say: educator / diagnostic
Don't say: salesman / sales pitch
Operators are protective of their time and skeptical of salespeople. Leading with "I'd love to run a diagnostic on your numbers" opens doors that "I want to pitch you something" slams shut. Same meeting, different category in the buyer's head.
Say: New → Known → Repeat
Don't say: acquisition / retention / re-engagement
The academic terms are forgettable. The Oddle spine is memorable and operator-readable. Say the same words every time — so every rep, every deck, every report uses the same language and merchants start thinking in it too.
Say: restaurant growth platform
Don't say: restaurant tech / F&B SaaS
"Tech" and "SaaS" put us in a crowded bucket. "Growth platform" puts us in a category the merchant cares about — growing the restaurant — and hints at the partner positioning underneath.
Say: Oddle is NOT a marketplace
Don't say: Oddle is like GrabFood, but…
Never anchor to the category we're trying to displace. Anchoring legitimises the comparison. State the difference flatly, then move to promised land.

Check for understanding

Before advancing to Module 4, a rep should be able to answer all of the below with no materials in front of them:

  1. Deliver the 30-second Oddle one-liner word-for-word. Then deliver the 90-second version in five beats.
  2. Name the enemy. State the three traps inside it. Give a one-sentence example of a merchant sitting in each trap.
  3. Describe the promised land in the five-step pipeline (multi-channel in → dashboard → insight → recommended action → automation) without reading it.
  4. State the three stages of New → Known → Repeat and the job the restaurant needs done in each. Not a product — the job.
  5. Explain the compounding loop in New → Known → Repeat — why a restaurant running the full loop pulls away from one running only a part.
  6. Name the six products in the Integrated Revenue System. For each, state which NKR stage(s) it contributes to — and why. (Warning: most products span multiple stages. A rep who says "Shop is only New" is wrong.)
  7. Walk through the product × stage matrix from memory: which products touch New, which touch Known, which touch Repeat. Then explain Virtual Lounge — why is it a New-stage product and not a Known one?
  8. State the four differentiators. For each, give the one-sentence proof for why it's true.
  9. A merchant says "I already use GrabFood, I don't need Oddle Shop." Respond in under 45 seconds without disparaging GrabFood.
  10. A merchant says "I have Chope already." Respond without positioning Oddle as "cheaper."
  11. State the four never-break language rules (eShop not online ordering, partner not vendor, educator not salesman, Oddle is not a marketplace). For each, explain why in one sentence.
  12. Name the three placeholder proof wins (New, Known, Repeat) and explain what the 45-second story structure looks like. (When proof is locked: deliver all three cold, with numbers.)
Drill — the 90-second cold delivery. Rep stands up, no slides, no notes, and delivers the full narrative: enemy → promised land → integrated revenue system → proof → ask. Manager rates on five criteria: did they name the enemy correctly? did they paint the promised land in operator-readable language? did they frame the six products as one system, not a list? did they use one named proof point? did they end with an ask? Any miss = re-drill until clean.
