Why We Demo

The demo is proof — not selling. The merchant already agrees our solution makes sense. They haven't signed yet. They want to see the system before they decide. The demo is what turns "this sounds right" into "let's do it."
3 min read

When a Demo Happens

A demo is not the start of the sale. It's the last step before they sign — the part that turns "yes, this sounds right" into "ok, let's do it." Run a demo only after three things are true:

#What must already be agreedWhere it was earned
1The merchant has agreed on their gaps and opportunities — and wants to solve them.M7 · Discovery + Get Buy-In
2The merchant has seen our intro and the All Access Plan — and agrees it's the right direction.M7 · Intro
3The merchant wants to see how it actually works before they sign.M7 · Transition to Demo
If any of these three is missing, you are not demoing. You are still pitching. Stop. Go back to M7. A demo run before the merchant agrees is just a feature tour — and feature tours don't close deals.
Important — the merchant has not said yes yet. They agree the direction is right. Now they want to see it before they sign. The demo's job is to move them from "sounds right" to "let's go." Don't treat the demo like a victory lap. It's the last thing that gets them to sign.

What the Demo Is For

One job. Show them it's real — and that they can see themselves running it.

"You've agreed this is the right direction for your business. Let me show you exactly how it works — so you can see it before you decide."

Everything before the demo was talk — gaps, leakages, the All Access Plan on paper. The demo is where the talk becomes real software the merchant can see, click through, and picture themselves using next week. Do this part well and the close is easy.

Inside a part, you can skip things that don't apply to their business. For example — don't demo Reserve to a fried-chicken QSR. But you can't skip a whole part. Use this table to decide:

The Core Flow

Three parts, always in this order. Touchpoints → Enrolment → CDP + Email. At the end of Part 3 you say: "That's what Oddle is."
12 min read
Before you open any tab. You should already know the business from M7. Open Reserve tabs if the merchant takes bookings. Open eShop tabs if they do delivery or takeaway. Open both if they do both. Skip what doesn't apply — but never skip Enrolment or CDP + Email. (See skip table in Chapter 1.)
1

Discovery Touchpoints New Known

Show where a diner first meets the restaurant. Two touchpoints: eShop (online order) and Reserve (reservation). Demo whichever applies. If both, demo both.

eShop — The Customer Journey

Skip this if: the merchant is fine dining or has clearly said they won't do delivery or takeaway. Example — an omakase that only serves diners in the seat. Don't demo eShop to someone who's ruled it out. Go straight to Reserve.

Open a live partner eShop. Walk through it from the diner's side — browse menu, add to cart, check out. Then show the two features that make eShop more than a menu page.

Key features to cover

  • Variants and options — size, add-ons, modifiers. Show how one menu item can carry full customisation and still order cleanly.
  • Promos — discount codes, bundles, time-limited offers. Show how the merchant runs a promo themselves without calling IT.

Reserve — The Customer Journey

Skip this if: the merchant is QSR, fast casual, fried chicken, counter-service, or cloud kitchen — any place where diners don't book a table. Example — Jollibee doesn't take reservations. Don't demo Reserve to them. Go straight to Enrolment.

Open a live partner Reserve. Show a diner booking a table — from Google, from the restaurant's own site, from the Virtual Lounge. Then walk through the four things most merchants care about.

Key features to cover

  • Google Reserve — booking direct from Google search. Zero clicks to a third-party.
  • Normal reservation — the default flow from the restaurant's own site.
  • Reservations for different areas — bar, dining room, private room, terrace. Each area bookable on its own with its own rules.
  • Credit card hold / prepayment — for busy slots, omakase seats, tasting menus. Cuts no-shows.
Stay on the diner's side. In Part 1 you only show what a customer sees. Back-of-house and ops come later in Chapter 3 — and only if they ask.
2

Enrolment — Capture the Diner Known

This is the part most merchants have never seen done properly. Spend time here. Walk the full flow — don't shortcut.

"Most restaurants lose the diner the moment they walk out the door. Enrolment is how you keep them."

Why Enrolment Gets Its Own Part

eShop and Reserve only capture the diners who book or order online. The walk-in guest, the one who came because a friend brought them — they stay invisible. Unless you have a way to capture them right at the table.

Enrolment is new to most operators. They haven't seen it work before. That's why you go deeper here than anywhere else in the demo.

Walk the Full Flow

Walk it as one continuous loop, from the diner's point of view:

The full Enrolment flow

  • Trigger — the QR code or NFC on the table. Diner scans.
  • Landing — the Virtual Lounge opens. Menu, membership sign-up, survey, reward.
  • Membership — name, phone, email, birthday captured. Diner is now a known customer.
  • Survey — "How did you hear about us? First time here? What did you order? Would you recommend us?" Data you can measure instead of guess.
  • Reward — automatic voucher, usable within 90 days on their next visit. You've just booked their return.
  • Loop — every redemption triggers the next voucher. One-time diner becomes a regular, automatically.
Say the number. "Most restaurants know only about 25% of their diners. With Enrolment on every table, our operators typically know 60–70% within 90 days." If they raised this gap in M7, they'll remember the number.

CDP — Segment the Customers

Open the CDP. Show one customer profile — every booking, order, enrolment, visit, and spend — stitched into one record. Then show segmentation.

Show the merchant how to segment

  • By value — top 20% spenders, VIPs, one-time high-spend diners.
  • By frequency — weekly regulars, monthly, lapsed, never returned.
  • By channel — dine-in only, delivery only, both.
  • By source — Google, Instagram, walk-in, referral.
  • By recency — visited in last 30 days, 60 days, 90+ days lapsed.

Each of these is a segment. Each segment is a campaign. Which is where Email Marketing comes in.

Email Marketing — Broadcast + Automation

Open Email Marketing. Show both modes — broadcasts the merchant runs, and automations that run by themselves.

Broadcast

  • AI-generated — pick a segment, tell the AI what the campaign is about, it writes the email for you.
  • Self-generated — full editor, templates, preview.
  • Send to a segment, track opens and conversions, feed results back to the CDP.

Automation

  • Win back lapsed regulars — runs every Monday, finds anyone who hasn't come back in 60 days, skips those who already redeemed.
  • Birthday campaigns — auto-send a voucher one week before a diner's birthday.
  • Post-visit follow-up — survey or thank-you, fires automatically after every visit.
  • Set it up once. Runs forever.

That's What Oddle Is

Close the laptop lid half an inch. Look up. Say:

"That's what Oddle is. Touchpoints bring in new diners. Enrolment turns them into known customers. CDP and Email turn known customers into regulars. New → Known → Repeat — one system, one piece of software, running in your restaurant."

That's the end of the core demo. The merchant now has a clear picture of what they'd be running. Chapter 3 covers what usually comes next — the "how do I run this?" and "what does it cost?" questions that come up right before they sign.

Chapter 3

What They Ask Next

After the core flow, most merchants have two kinds of follow-up questions — operational ("how do I actually run this?") and costing ("what does this cost me?"). Show only what they ask for.
6 min read
Only show this if they ask. Unlike the core flow, Chapter 3 isn't compulsory. Open these tabs only when the merchant raises the question. Showing ops and costing when no one asked just adds time and hurts the close.

Operations Questions

Once they've seen the customer side, operators want to know how their team runs it. Three common questions — show each only if they ask.

Manage Orders

Open the eShop ops dashboard. Show how an order comes in, how it goes to the kitchen, how the team marks it ready, how the status goes back to the customer. Keep it short — most operators just need to see it's one clean screen.

What to show

  • New orders with their status
  • Kitchen print or KDS
  • Prep-time rules — adjustable for peak hours
  • Menu, pricing, promo control — live, no IT needed

Manage Logistics

For merchants running their own delivery or using a fleet. Show how delivery works end to end.

What to show

  • Delivery options — your own drivers, integrated fleet, or a mix
  • Zone and distance rules for delivery fees
  • Driver assignment and live tracking
  • Customer-facing tracking link

Manage Reservations

For merchants with a dine-in business. Show the Reserve dashboard from the host's point of view.

What to show

  • Today's bookings by time slot
  • Floor plan and table assignments by area
  • VIP flags, dietary notes, guest history on each booking
  • Walk-in capacity tracking

Costing

Most merchants want to see the numbers before they sign. For eShop, there's a tool for this — use it.

eShop Cost Calculator

Open the eShop cost calculator. Put in the merchant's own numbers — average online order value, orders per month, current marketplace commission. The calculator shows the saving vs marketplaces, line by line.

What the calculator shows

  • Current cost on marketplace — commission + fees per order × volume
  • Cost on Oddle eShop — platform fee + per-order costs
  • Net saving per month, per year
  • Break-even point — the month they start saving money from switching
Use their real numbers, not sample ones. The power is in the merchant seeing their own data. If they don't know their current marketplace cost, help them estimate from monthly GMV and the marketplace commission % — but always use their numbers, never a generic example.

Wrap-Up

After the core flow (Chapter 2) and any questions they raised (Chapter 3), you have nothing more to demo. What's left is the paperwork — confirm the plan, sign the agreement, set the start date. That's Module 5 (Products & Pricing) and the closing flow in M7 — not this module.

Don't keep demoing. When the merchant stops asking, the demo is done. Reps who keep clicking through tabs "just one more thing" hurt the close. The demo did its job — move to paperwork.
