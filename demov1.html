Chapter 1

Pre-Demo Discipline

The demo is won before you open Chrome. Confirm the archetype. Pick the variant. Set the frame. Reps who skip this lose the room in the first 90 seconds.
8 min read
Prerequisites: Modules 1–7. M6 earned the meeting. M7 ran Discovery and got Buy-In. This module is Stage 3 Intro → Stage 4 Present. If you're demoing before you have buy-in on the gaps, you're running a product tour — not a sales call. Go back.

Why We Built a Demo Playbook

Without one, reps default to a product feature tour. They open every tab, walk through every screen, lose the narrative, and end on "let me know what you think." The merchant leaves impressed — and never signs.

A demo is not a software demonstration. It's a merchant seeing their own future restaurant, with their own customers, through the Oddle system. The job is to make the New → Known → Repeat loop visible — not the UI.

"If your demo could be replaced by a screen recording, you shouldn't be running it."

The Four Non-Negotiables

Every demo — full or short, any archetype — obeys these four rules.

#RuleWhy it breaks if ignored
1NKR framing, always. Every product is positioned inside New → Known → Repeat. Never as a standalone tool.Merchant hears "another app." Methodology disappears. They compare feature-by-feature vs competitors and you lose.
2Merchant voice, not investor voice. We are not pitching a moat, a flywheel, or a TAM. We are pitching more covers, more captured diners, fewer marketplace dollars lost.Operator switches off. "Moat" and "compounding" mean nothing at 9pm on a Friday service.
3Skip what doesn't apply. Archetype dictates what you show. Showing Reserve to a delivery-only cloud kitchen burns credibility.Rep seems untailored. Merchant thinks "he's giving me the same pitch he gave everyone." Trust evaporates.
4End on the flywheel (Act 4), not on Ops (Act 5). CDP + Email is the why. Ops is the how. Always close the story on the why before you mention the how.Demo ends on backend screens. Merchant's last mental image is software admin — not revenue growth.

Full Demo vs Short Demo — Pick Before The Call

Two variants exist. Choose before the meeting based on the slot length the merchant agreed to. Never start a Full demo in a Short slot — you'll chop the wrong Act under time pressure and lose the flywheel close.

Full Demo

~30 min · 5 Acts · Default
  • All 5 Acts in sequence
  • 2–3 demo links per Act
  • Full narrative bridges between Acts
  • Merchant-voice translation of every product
  • Closes on Act 4 (flywheel) with Act 5 as operational proof

Use when: Merchant blocked ≥30 min · owner/GM present · M7 Buy-In secured on ≥3 gaps.

Short Demo

~15 min · 3 Acts · Archetype-branched
  • 3 Acts only — drops Act 5 (Ops) and compresses the side not matching archetype
  • 1 demo link per Act (the hero link)
  • No narrative atmosphere — straight Gap → Answer → What Changes
  • Still obeys NKR framing
  • Still closes on the flywheel

Use when: Merchant has 15–20 min only · follow-up meeting · or you've re-engaged a stalled deal.

The operational reason both exist. Reps currently wing the short version badly — they start the 30-min demo in a 20-min slot, run over, chop Ops and the flywheel in panic, and end on product features. A pre-built short variant pre-commits you to the right cuts. Ops goes. The flywheel stays.

The Pre-Demo Checklist

Run this 15 minutes before every demo. If you can't answer a line, go back to M6 prospecting notes or ping your manager.

  • Archetype confirmed — which of the 4 is this restaurant? (Delivery-marketplace only · Marketplace+Reserve · Reserve-only · eShop+Reserve)
  • Variant chosen — Full or Short, locked before the call
  • Top 2 gaps from M7 Buy-In named — you know which gaps the merchant has explicitly agreed to
  • Lead wedge identified — which Act leads this specific demo (the one aligned to their biggest gap)
  • Demo links pre-loaded — tabs open, pinned, tested 5 min before (do not fumble in front of the merchant)
  • Attio updated — deal moved to Stage 3 Intro or Stage 4 Present, notes from M7 logged
  • Close ask rehearsed — you know exactly what First Close you're asking for at the end

Chapter 2

The Full Demo · 30 min

Five Acts. Each Act is a Gap → Oddle answer → What changes. Merchant voice throughout — never investor voice. End the story on Act 4, not Act 5.
20 min read
How to read this chapter. Each Act shows: (a) the merchant problem you're solving, (b) the exact opening line you say, (c) what you click through, (d) the "what changes for you" line that anchors the proof. Memorize the transitions — they carry the narrative from one Act to the next.

The Opening Frame · 60 seconds

Before you touch any tab. Set the frame. This is the bridge from M7 Buy-In into the demo.

Opening Frame
You just spent 20 minutes telling me about these gaps — you're missing data on 75% of your diners, you can't tell where customers come from, and you have no way to pull lapsed regulars back. I want to show you how a restaurant using our system actually runs. Not software screens — actual restaurants, actual customers, from the day they discover the place to the day they come back for the fifth time.
Why it works: anchors the demo in their gaps (M7 output), sets the narrative expectation (customer journey, not UI), pre-empts the "feature tour" frame.
1Capture

Discovery → First Visit

~5 min

The gap this Act closes: Restaurants spend on Google, Instagram, TikTok — but 70%+ of that traffic leaks to marketplaces, Chope, or just disappears. The restaurant never captures the diner as its own.

"Imagine a customer Googles your restaurant for dinner tonight. Where does that click go?"
Beat 1 · Google search

They Google the restaurant — and the first thing they see is a direct-booking CTA

Here's what a diner sees when they Google your restaurant today. [Open Google search for a partner like Beyond the Dough.] Notice — the Reserve button isn't Chope. It's direct. Every booking made here is yours. No commission. No intermediary. The diner's name, phone, email — all captured straight into your database.
Beat 2 · Instagram / TikTok

Social funnels to the Virtual Lounge, not a marketplace

Your Instagram bio has one link. Today, it probably goes to Chope or a Linktree with dead links. Look at this instead — [open the partner Virtual Lounge] — think of this as your restaurant's Linktree, but built for F&B. Videos of your signature dishes. Reels from last weekend's service. One-tap CTAs to Reserve, to order online, to see the menu, to join the membership. Even the table-QR for walk-ins sits here. One page. Every channel converts.
Why the Virtual Lounge matters here: It's the single destination the restaurant controls. Every ad, every QR, every social bio points here. Zero leakage to competitors.
Beat 3 · Transition out

What just changed for you

So the first thing that changes — every diner who discovers you, no matter the channel, lands on a page you own. Not Chope's. Not Google's. Yours. And because the booking is direct, you have their data the moment they confirm. That's how you start closing the 75% data gap you mentioned.
2Enrol the Room

First Visit → Known Customer

~6 min

The gap this Act closes: Even when a diner walks in, you never capture them. They sit, eat, pay, leave — and they stay a stranger. Most restaurants know 25% of their diners at best.

"Let's say they walked in without a booking. How do you turn a walk-in stranger into a known customer, without asking your service staff to run a spreadsheet?"
Beat 1 · QR / NFC on the table

The table is the capture surface

On every table, there's a QR code or NFC tap. The diner scans. [Open the Virtual Lounge from the diner's phone view.] They land on your Virtual Lounge — the same one from Instagram. But now they're in-house, with food coming. They see: menu, a survey, a membership sign-up, rewards. Three taps and they're a member.
Beat 2 · The three-asset loop

Membership + Survey + Rewards — designed to compound

Three things happen inside this.
  One — Membership sign-up. Name, phone, email, birthday. You now own the customer.
  Two — Survey. "How did you hear about us? Are you a first-timer? What did you order? How likely would you recommend us?" You learn acquisition source, visit type, satisfaction — all measured, not gut-feel.
  Three — Reward. A voucher, automatic, redeemable on their next visit within 90 days. Their first-return is now pre-committed.
The 90-day loop. First visit → voucher → 90 days → redeem → new voucher. Every redemption restarts the cycle. This is how a one-time diner becomes a regular — automatically, without your managers having to remember anything.
Beat 3 · Transition out

What just changed for you

The 75% of diners who used to walk out as strangers? Now they walk out as named members — with a reason to come back in 90 days. That's the Data Gap, the Insight Gap, and the Engagement Gap, all closed by one QR sticker on the table.
3Online Orders

Off-Premise Revenue, Without the 30% Commission

~5 min

The gap this Act closes: 30–40% of F&B spend now happens off-premise. Most restaurants funnel it through GrabFood or FoodPanda at 25–35% commission — and lose the customer data to the marketplace.

"When someone wants to order from you tonight but doesn't want to visit — where do they go? Does that customer belong to you or to GrabFood?"
Beat 1 · Oddle Shop visible everywhere

Your own ordering channel — on Google, on social, on your site

Here's what a customer ordering tonight sees. [Google search → partner Oddle Shop link.] Direct ordering from your own menu. No marketplace branding. No 30% commission. Every order is a direct relationship — name, phone, address, delivery history — all yours.
Beat 2 · Where the shop shows up

Three channels, one Shop

The Shop lives in three places.
  Your Google Business Profile — so when a hungry customer searches "sushi near me," your direct Shop is one tap away.
  Your Facebook and Instagram — "Order Now" button goes to Shop, not a marketplace.
  Your website and Virtual Lounge — embedded directly. Corporate orders, group orders, delivery — all captured in one flow.
Beat 3 · The margin math

What changes for you on unit economics

If 30% of your online orders shift from marketplace to direct — at a typical $30 AOV and 28% commission saved — that's real net margin, not top-line vanity. And direct ordering unlocks the bigger-ticket customer too: families, offices, corporates — the $100+ orders that never make it to a marketplace.
4Flywheel

Data → Insight → Engagement → Repeat

~7 min

The gap this Act closes: Even if the restaurant captures data, it sits dead. Five siloed systems (reservation, POS, delivery, loyalty, Excel) never talk to each other. No unified view of who the customer is. No automated campaigns. Gut feel runs the business.

This is the Act that closes the deal. The merchant sees their own customer data, unified, segmented, and turned into revenue. Every previous Act was input into this one. Do not rush it.
"Everything we've looked at so far captures customers — first visit, walk-in, online order. Now let's look at what you do with them once they're known."
Beat 1 · Unified profile

One customer. One record. Every touchpoint.

Here's a customer who booked for their anniversary, walked in three more times, ordered delivery on two rainy nights, redeemed a birthday voucher. [Open CDP customer profile in sandbox.] Today in your system, that customer is at least three different records in three different tools. In Oddle, it's one profile. Every interaction — stitched together automatically. You see spend, frequency, channel, lifecycle stage, preferences, last visit. One source of truth.
Beat 2 · Segmentation

You can now ask questions of your customer base

Now you can ask questions you couldn't ask before.
  — Who are my top 20% spenders — and when were they last here?
  — Who came once and never returned in 90 days?
  — Who discovered me through Instagram versus walk-in?
  — Who only orders delivery and has never dined in?
  Each of these is a segment. Each segment is a campaign.
Beat 3 · Automated engagement

Email + SMS campaigns that run themselves

Pick a segment — say, VIPs who haven't been in for 60 days. [Open Email Marketing in sandbox.] Draft, one-click preview, schedule. The system sends, tracks opens, tracks conversions, and feeds redemption data back into the CDP. Next week's campaign automatically excludes everyone who already redeemed. The lapsed-regular reactivation campaign runs every Monday — you don't touch it again after setting it up.
Beat 4 · The close line

The flywheel

This is the flywheel. Every new diner feeds the data. Every insight feeds the next campaign. Every campaign produces more repeat visits — which produce more data. It compounds. Six months in, your marketing spend drops because your own database does the work. That's the business we're helping you build.
5Ops · Table Stakes

The Backend — Quick Tour Only

~4 min

Why Act 5 is last: Ops is table stakes, not a wedge. The merchant only cares after they've bought into the growth story. Show it briefly to prove operational credibility — do not open the demo here, do not close here.

Discipline check. You have about 4 minutes. Show the Reserve merchant dashboard + the Shop ops dashboard. Don't click into every sub-tab. If the operator asks for more, schedule a follow-up — don't derail the close.
Beat 1 · Reserve dashboard

Bookings, walk-ins, floor plan — all in one view

This is what your manager sees every morning. Today's bookings by time slot. Walk-in capacity. Table assignments. VIP flags on the booking cards. Notes on dietary restrictions. It runs on an iPad at the host stand — one operational view of the whole room.
Beat 2 · Shop ops

Orders, delivery, prep times — one dashboard

On the Shop side — orders come in here, auto-routed to the kitchen printer. Delivery dispatch integrated. Prep-time rules you can adjust for peak hours. Menu control, pricing, promo codes — all live. Your ops team doesn't need to learn three systems.
Beat 3 · The return-to-close

Bring it back to the flywheel

So — the customer-facing side captures and engages. The ops side runs the floor and the orders. And every action on either side feeds the same customer profile in the CDP. The flywheel I just showed you — it's the glue holding these two sides together. That's what makes this a system, not a stack of tools.
Why the return-to-close matters. Ops is the last thing they see. Without the return-to-close line, their last mental image is admin screens. With it, you re-anchor to the flywheel — the reason they're going to say yes.

The Short Demo · 15 min

Three Acts, not five. Archetype-branched. The rule: preserve NKR framing, preserve the flywheel close. Drop Ops. Compress the side that doesn't match the archetype.
10 min read

When to Use the Short Demo

This variant is not a "worse Full Demo." It's a different tool for different meetings.

ScenarioUse ShortUse Full
First demo with an operatorIf they only blocked 15–20 minIf they blocked ≥30 min
Re-engaging a stalled dealDefault. They've already seen the full picture.Only if there's a new decision-maker
Second meeting after an internal demo to the teamDefault. Drill into the Act that needs alignment.Rarely — they've seen it
Group audience (investors, HQ leadership)No — use the investor-voice flow insteadNot this module at all

The Three-Act Cuts, by Archetype

Delivery-marketplace only → Acts 3 · 2 · 4

Why: They have no dine-in capture problem — they don't have a dining room. Start on Shop (their biggest current leak to GrabFood). Bridge to Enrol-via-QR on the delivery packaging. End on the flywheel.

Sequence
Act 3 · Shop (5 min) — lead wedge. Direct ordering, commission math, your own customer data. Skip the dine-in context.
Act 2 · Enrol (4 min) — QR goes on the takeaway bag/receipt. Same 3-asset loop (Membership + Survey + Rewards) but triggered post-delivery.
Act 4 · Flywheel (5 min) — CDP unifies delivery + social + web. Email campaigns target lapsed-ordering customers. Close.
Skip: Act 1 (no walk-in discovery to solve) · Act 5 (no floor ops).

Marketplace + Reservation → Acts 3 · 2 · 4

Why: Shop is usually the biggest immediate wedge — they're already bleeding to marketplaces. Reserve is already working somewhat (via Chope or similar), so Enrol is where the data leverage compounds. Close on flywheel.

Sequence
Act 3 · Shop (5 min) — lead. Same margin math, direct ordering, 3 channels.
Act 2 · Enrol (4 min) — QR/NFC on tables + takeaway. 3-asset loop capturing both dine-in and delivery diners.
Act 4 · Flywheel (5 min) — unified CDP across reservation (Chope replacement), Shop, walk-in, delivery.
Skip: Act 1 (quick mention of "your Google/Instagram bookings become direct" but don't open links) · Act 5.

Reservation only → Acts 1 · 2 · 4

Why: No off-premise motion yet — they don't have a delivery or takeaway line. Open on Capture (Reserve is their known strength). Bridge to Enrol (the big unlock for them). End on flywheel.

Sequence
Act 1 · Capture (5 min) — lead. Google + Instagram → Reserve direct, no commission. Virtual Lounge as their operator-owned hub.
Act 2 · Enrol (5 min) — QR/NFC on the table. 3-asset loop. This is where the biggest data unlock lives for this archetype.
Act 4 · Flywheel (4 min) — CDP on reservations + walk-ins. Email to lapsed VIPs. Close.
Skip: Act 3 (optional 30-sec teaser: "when you're ready to add online ordering, Shop slots in on the same system — but let's focus on the data foundation today") · Act 5.

eShop + Reservation → Acts 2 · 1 · 4 (Enrol leads)

Why: Both sides of the house exist. The gap is the unified data layer and the membership/engagement loop. Lead with Enrol (the glue), then Capture (show how the Virtual Lounge unifies both channels), close on flywheel.

Sequence
Act 2 · Enrol (5 min) — lead. QR/NFC + Virtual Lounge. This is the glue — both channels feed here.
Act 1 · Capture (4 min) — Virtual Lounge as one hub for Reserve + eShop + table-QR. Show unification.
Act 4 · Flywheel (5 min) — CDP across reservations, Shop, walk-ins. Show the one-customer-one-profile view.
Skip: Act 3 (they already have a Shop — quick acknowledgement but don't re-demo) · Act 5.

Short-Demo Discipline

Four rules specific to the Short variant. Break any one and you've just run a bad Full demo in a short slot.

RuleWhy
1. One demo link per Act. Maximum.Multiple links blow past 5 minutes per Act and ruin the pacing.
2. No narrative atmosphere.Skip the "Imagine a customer Googles you…" opener. Go straight into Gap → Open demo → What changes.
3. No Act 5.Ever. Ops can be a follow-up meeting. Losing the flywheel close is not worth showing a backend screen.
4. Still close on Act 4.Non-negotiable. The flywheel is the reason they'll say yes. No shortcut removes this.
If you find yourself over 15 minutes on Act 1 or Act 2, you are running a bad Full demo. Stop. Breathe. Compress to the single demo link and move to Act 4. Running over on the Short kills the close worse than skipping a beat.

Chapter 4

Mid-Demo Objections

The objections that surface during the demo — not after. Handle them without breaking pacing. This is not the full objection library (that lives in Chapter 5 cross-ref); these are the ones that interrupt a demo.
12 min read
How to handle a mid-demo objection. Acknowledge → reframe → answer in ≤30 seconds → return to the Act. If you need more than 30 seconds, say "great question, let me park that and come back — it connects to Act 4." Pacing > completeness.

Category 1 · Price & Value

1. "This looks expensive. What does it cost?"

Said during: any Act. Usually triggered when Shop or Reserve is opened.
Reframe to unit economics. "The All-Access plan starts around $215/month — but the real number is what you save. Restaurants on our system typically shift 30–40% of their marketplace delivery to direct. At a 28% commission saved on $5k/month of delivery, the system pays for itself in the first week. Let's keep going — I'll come back to the pricing detail with the proposal."

2. "I can do most of this myself with Mailchimp + Chope + GrabFood."

Said during: Act 4 (Flywheel), usually right when you open the CDP.
Agree then reveal the glue. "You absolutely can — and many restaurants do. The reason they come to us is not because they can't stitch it together. It's because after six months of stitching, the data still doesn't unify. A customer who books on Chope and orders on GrabFood looks like two people. What we just showed you is one profile per customer across all touchpoints. That's the piece nobody sells as a standalone."

Category 2 · Competitive

3. "We're already on Chope / SevenRooms."

Said during: Act 1 (Capture).
Don't bash — reposition. "Chope is a great channel — they'll bring you bookings. What's not in their design is the rest: QR-to-membership, data unification, lapsed-regular automation, direct Shop. We're designed as the operating system. You can keep Chope as a top-of-funnel if you want, and we replace Chope's reservation feature with our direct Reserve — every booking becomes commission-free and first-party."

4. "We just want the Shop part — the rest we don't need."

Said during: Act 3, after Shop lands well and before you get to Flywheel.
Don't let them buy half. "I hear you — and Shop alone will pay for itself. But let me show you what happens to your Shop customers when they're in the CDP" — [jump into Act 4 now, skip to the segment view] — "this is the same delivery customer, but segmented by frequency. This is why our restaurants renew — Shop is the wedge, the flywheel is the reason they grow."

5. "GrabFood / FoodPanda brings us new customers — you don't."

Said during: Act 3.
Validate then reframe. "Correct — marketplaces are discovery engines. We're not replacing them for new customers. We're replacing them for repeat customers. Once a marketplace diner has ordered once, every repeat order through them costs you 28%. Our Shop captures that repeat — at zero commission. Marketplaces for new. Oddle for known and repeat."

Category 3 · Adoption & Effort

6. "My team won't use this. They can't even use our current POS properly."

Said during: Act 5 (Ops), or Act 2 when QR/NFC is introduced.
Reframe from learning curve to automation. "That's exactly why this isn't more work for them. The QR on the table does the capture — your staff don't do anything. The voucher sends automatically. The campaign runs automatically. We don't give your team more tasks — we remove the tasks they currently do manually. Host-stand onboarding is 90 minutes."

7. "How long does it take to set up?"

Said during: usually late Act 4 or Act 5.
Give the real answer, then bridge to urgency. "Typical go-live is 3–4 weeks end-to-end — menu and Shop built, Reserve configured, Enrolment campaign mapped. The flywheel needs 90 days of data before it's compounding. Which is why restaurants that sign today are ahead of competitors who sign in 90 days. Time is the asset in this category."

Category 4 · Skepticism & Stall

8. "Show me proof. Which restaurants actually grew revenue from this?"

Said during: end of Act 4, or after Act 5 close.
Name 2, not 10. Specific to their archetype. "Sure — let me pick two similar to you. [Name 1] is a [same archetype] that grew repeat visits from X% to Y% in six months. [Name 2] shifted 40% of their delivery off marketplace in the first quarter. I can send the detailed case next to the proposal — want me to put both in?"

9. "Let me show this to my partner/head office/owner first."

Said during: any time after Act 4.
Identify the real decision-maker, co-present with them. "Totally — this is a big enough decision that they should see it. Rather than forwarding a deck, let's do a 30-minute session with them. I'll send three slots this week — which one works?" (Do not let them take a static deck and walk. The deck won't convert; you will.)

10. "I need to think about it / send me a proposal."

Said at the end of the demo.
Don't accept a soft no. "Happy to send the proposal — but before I do, help me understand what you need to think about specifically. Is it the investment? The setup timeline? Something specific from today's demo? I'd rather answer the real question now than wait for a follow-up that goes cold." (This is the bridge into Chapter 5 — Demo → First Close.)

The One Rule When You Don't Know the Answer

Never make it up. Say: "I don't have the exact number — let me get it from the team and include it in the proposal I send you within 24 hours." Then send it within 24 hours. One honest "I don't know" builds more trust than five half-right answers.

Chapter 5

Demo → First Close

The two minutes after the demo ends are the highest-leverage two minutes of the entire process. Don't fumble them. Don't end on "so what do you think?" Here's the exact bridge.
7 min read

Why Most Demos Don't Close

Reps finish Act 4, feel the energy in the room is good, and default to: "So — what do you think?" The operator says: "Looks great, send me a proposal." The deal goes to follow-up, cools for 10 days, and dies.

"A demo doesn't end. It transitions. If you let it end, the deal dies in follow-up."

The 90-Second Close Bridge

Run this the moment you finish Act 4 (Full) or Act 4 (Short). Every time.

Step 1 · Summarise in their words (20 sec)
So — to recap what you said at the start of this call. You said you don't know 75% of your diners, you can't tell where customers come from, and you have no way to reactivate regulars. What we just walked through — Capture, Enrol, Shop, Flywheel — is the system that closes all three. Specifically for you, the biggest unlock is [Act N — their lead wedge].
Step 2 · Test the agreement (15 sec)
Before I talk about next steps — does what I just showed you solve the problem you came into this meeting with?
If YES: go to Step 3. If HESITATION: "What's missing?" — then handle the actual objection (Chapter 4) before moving on. Do not move to close over a hesitation.
Step 3 · The First Close ask (30 sec)
Good. Here's what I'd recommend. Most restaurants in your shoes start with [named wedge — eShop / Reserve / Enrolment-led All-Access]. Setup is 3–4 weeks. If we kick off this month, you're live by [specific date] and the flywheel starts compounding. I can send the proposal today, lock the kick-off date on our calendar, and we sign the agreement this week. Does that work for you?
Key: ask for a specific, time-bound commitment. Not "is that interesting?" — "Does that work for you?" The latter demands a yes or a specific objection.
Step 4 · Read the answer (25 sec)
Three possible answers. Each has its own script.

Answer A — "Yes, let's do it"

Script
Excellent. I'll send the paperwork and proposal within the hour. I'll also pencil in [date] for kick-off. My colleague [AM/CSM name] will be in touch by end of week to schedule onboarding. Welcome to Oddle.
Immediately in Attio: Stage → Verbal Agreed. Proposal out within 60 min. Follow-up meeting booked before you leave this one.

Answer B — "Yes, but I need [specific thing]"

Script
Absolutely — what specifically do you need? Is it a decision-maker confirmation, a team-level review, a specific data point, or something commercial? Let's figure out the exact next step right now so this doesn't stall.
Goal: get them to name the thing. Then book the meeting that resolves the thing before you leave this meeting. Never leave with an open-ended "I'll let you know."

Answer C — "I need time / send the proposal"

Script
Of course — happy to send the proposal. Two things before I do. One — what's the specific thing you want to think about? Because if I can answer it now, you'll get to the decision faster. Two — when would be a good time to regroup? I'll hold [date/time] for us. If you've made the decision before then, great — but we both have it on the calendar.
The two gates: (1) surface the real objection now, not in a cold follow-up. (2) Force a scheduled follow-up — never a vague "let me think about it." If they refuse to book a follow-up, log in Attio: stall risk.

Before your first live demo with a merchant, you should be able to answer all of these from memory. If you can't, re-read the relevant chapter.

  1. Name the four non-negotiable rules of every Oddle demo. Why does each exist?
  2. The merchant is a delivery-marketplace-only cloud kitchen. You have 15 minutes. Which Acts do you show, which do you skip, and in what order?
  3. Why does Act 5 (Ops) always go last, never first?
  4. Walk through the 3-asset loop in Act 2. What are the three assets, what do they capture, and why does it compound?
