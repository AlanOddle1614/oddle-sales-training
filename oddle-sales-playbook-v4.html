Sales Philosophy

Diagnose, Don't Pitch

A doctor doesn't walk into the room and say "you need surgery." They ask questions, run tests, and help the patient understand what's happening. Then the patient asks for help. That's what we do.

You are not here to sell software. You are here to help a restaurant owner see — with their own numbers and their own words — where they're leaking revenue, overpaying, or flying blind. The pitch becomes inevitable because the merchant already sees the problem.

Questions Drive Insight, Not Statements

When you tell a merchant "you have a data gap," they can disagree. When you ask "how many of your 5,000 diners do you actually have contact info for?" and they calculate 500 — they can't disagree with their own math. The question is always more powerful than the statement.

The 5-Step Pre-Pitch Process

Before any product is mentioned, every call moves through these steps:

  1. Discover Intent — why are they here?
  2. Discover Gaps & Opportunities, establish anchors — Socratic questioning adapted to their profile
  3. Summarize & Confirm — mirror the gaps back, merchant agrees
  4. Get Buy-In — merchant confirms they want to resolve these gaps
  5. Then and only then → General Introduction & Demo

The Five Gaps — What You're Looking For

Every restaurant has some combination of these five gaps. Your job during the diagnostic is to find which ones apply, quantify them, and help the merchant see them clearly. Memorize this table — it's your mental checklist.

GapWhat It MeansThe Key Question
Data GapMost customers are invisible. No contact info, no way to bring them back. Reservations only capture the booker — not the group."How many diners per month? How many do you actually have data on?"
Insight GapEven with some data, they don't know WHY customers come — which channel, how far they travel, what drives return visits."Do you know how your customers discover you?"
Engagement GapData sits idle. They're not segmenting, not targeting, not doing anything to drive repeat visits."What are you doing today to bring customers back?"
Revenue GapMissing entire sales channels — corporate, group, direct delivery — because they only work with marketplaces or don't have online ordering."Do you think customers order for families, offices, groups? Are you capturing those?"
Operational GapTools are fragmented, expensive, or underperforming. No visibility into traffic, conversion, or logistics."Do you know your online traffic? Conversion rate?"
The sequence is flexible. The thoroughness is not. You don't always go Data → Insight → Engagement → Revenue → Operations. You lead with whatever the merchant cares about most. But by the end of the diagnostic, you should have covered all five. Think of them as a checklist, not a script.

Two Things You Must Do Throughout Every Step

1. Establish that Oddle truly knows F&B. Every question you ask should carry an embedded insight about the restaurant business. The merchant should walk away thinking "these people really know their stuff."

2. Give value along the way. Drop small tips that help the merchant even if they don't sign up — how to improve Google ranking, why Google reviews matter, how email frequency drives recall. Each tip builds trust and proves you're a revenue partner, not a tool vendor. See Value Tips Library for a bank of these.

Always Lead with the Big Picture

When you do introduce Oddle, start with the overall methodology — New → Known → Repeat — and how Oddle helps restaurants grow revenue organically. Never lead with a specific product. eShop, Reserve, Enrolment — these are parts of the ecosystem. Presenting any one as THE solution reduces Oddle to a tool instead of a revenue growth system.

Know Your Merchant

Every merchant is different. The pitch flow is always the same 10 steps — but within those steps, what you emphasise, what questions you dig into, and which products you lead with changes based on three dimensions. Learn these. You'll be reading these situations in real time during every call.

1

Rapport

~1 min
"How's business?"

That's it. Light rapport — food, familiarity if you've been to the restaurant, a genuine compliment if something stood out. Then transition quickly.

Common mistake: New reps spend 5–10 minutes on rapport because they're nervous about the diagnostic. One minute is enough. The best rapport is built through a great diagnostic conversation, not small talk.

3

Set the Diagnostic Frame

~30 sec

Ask permission. Position yourself as a consultant, not a salesperson.

"Before I share more about what we do, I'd love to understand your business first — where the gaps might be, where the opportunities are. Would it be okay if I ask you a few questions?"

This does three things: it gives you control of the conversation, positions you as someone here to understand (not demo), and gets their explicit permission to be open.

If they say "just show me what you have": Go into the General Introduction (Step 7), but weave diagnostic questions into the presentation as you go. "This is our reservation product — by the way, are you using one currently? How many bookings?" You're still diagnosing, just inside the presentation.
4

The Diagnostic

~10-15 min

This is the core of the entire system. Below are the exact questions to ask, organized into five probes. Your intent discovery (Step 2) tells you which probe to start with, but aim to cover all relevant areas by the time you reach the Summary.

Seasoned rep move — Offer to Check Together. When a merchant doesn't have numbers handy, offer: "Do you mind if we take a quick look at your system right now?" This turns the meeting into a working session and builds massive trust. You're not just asking questions — you're helping them see their own data. Look for: total bookings, online vs manual split, return customer %, monthly GMV, traffic, conversion rate, new members vs total diners. Note: most merchants who genuinely "don't know" also can't log into their systems on the spot — so this works best when you sense the data exists but they just haven't checked recently.

The Capacity Question

"Are you at maximum capacity?"

This question is a fork in the road. The answer changes what you sell and how you frame it.

If they say...What it means for your pitch
"We're packed every night" / "We sell out daily"You can't sell them more covers. But you CAN sell them: (1) eShop to solve logistics for takeaway/delivery they're already doing, (2) Enrolment to capture data NOW during peak — because when the hype fades, they'll need that database to bring people back, (3) CDP + Email to activate that database. Frame it: "You're full now — but are you capturing these customers so you can reach them when things slow down?"
"Weekdays are slow" / "Lunch is empty"Clear growth opportunity. Data capture + proximity targeting is the play: "If you knew which customers live or work within 2km, you could send them a midweek lunch offer. 1,000 emails → 300 opens → 50 claims → 30 extra covers on your quietest day."
"We're doing okay but want to grow"Broad opportunity. Run the full diagnostic across all five gaps. They're open to everything.
Why this matters: A packed restaurant and a struggling restaurant need completely different pitches. Packed = capture and retain. Struggling = drive traffic and repeat visits. Getting this wrong means pitching growth to someone who doesn't need more covers, or pitching retention to someone who doesn't have enough customers to retain.

5

The Summary

~2 min

Mirror back what the merchant told you. Their language, their numbers. This turns the diagnostic into a mandate.

Move 1: Acknowledge

"Thank you for sharing all of that. Everything you've told me is really important — it gives me a much clearer picture of where your business is and where the potential is."

Move 2: Stack the gaps — lead with revenue

Always start with revenue growth. That's what every merchant cares about most. Then layer the other gaps.

"From what you've shared, I see a few things.

First, there's a clear growth opportunity — [tailor: 'those corporate and group orders you're getting ad-hoc could be a real revenue channel' OR 'driving more return visits from your existing customers'].

But to do that effectively, you need the data. Right now, from the [X] customers you serve monthly, you only know [Y] of them. That's [Z]% walking out invisible — you can't bring them back, engage them, or even tell who your regulars are.

[If applicable: 'And you're paying $[total] across [N] tools that don't talk to each other.']

So the opportunity is real — the infrastructure to capture it isn't there yet."
Always use their numbers, not yours. "From your 5,000 diners and 500 bookings" — not "most restaurants have this problem." Their numbers make it personal and undeniable.
Summary adaptation for tech-stack merchants

If cost was the theme: "You're paying $X for tool A and $Y for tool B — $Z total for just two functions. And from what you've shared, they're not fully delivering on growth."

If value was the theme: "You have the tools, but the data is siloed, the engagement isn't happening, and the sales channel isn't performing to its potential."

If they couldn't answer questions (no numbers): "What stands out is that you have tools in place but don't have full visibility into how they're performing. And it sounds like your current providers haven't been helping you figure that out either."

Move 3: Confirm agreement

"Does that sound about right? Are these the main areas where you see opportunity?"
Wait for them to say yes. This is critical. They must explicitly agree on the gaps before you move forward. If they push back, adjust. If they add something you missed, acknowledge it. Do not proceed until you have agreement.
6

NEW

"So based on what we've discussed — you want to [grow online sales / capture more customer data / drive repeat visits / lower your tech costs]. If we have a system that addresses these gaps, is that something you'd want to explore?"

You're asking a simple question: if I can solve what you just told me is broken, do you want to hear about it?

Buy-in for tech-stack merchants

Buy-in for new businesses

"You're building your foundation right now. If there's a system that captures every customer from day one and grows with you — would that be worth looking at?"

Never demo when they're ready to buy. A demo at this point gives them time to find things to worry about. The best meetings end with a handshake, not a screen share.

Objection Handling

"Your eShop is more expensive than what I have"

Angle 1 — Surface their value gap: "Can your eShop sales be higher?" / "Are you having logistics issues?" / "Is your eShop data unified with dine-in?" — almost always no on all counts.

Angle 2 — Growth math: "Your eShop does $10K/month. The difference is 6.5% above $3K — about $455. If we drive even $1K more through abandoned cart recovery, corporate orders, and Oddle Eats — you've covered it."

Angle 3 — Reframe: "Don't compare eShop to eShop. Compare your total tech stack to Oddle. $215 for everything unified vs $X across disconnected tools."

"I already have a loyalty program"

Probe value: "How many new members per month? What's the return rate? Are you using the data?" Usually the data sits idle. "The program isn't the problem — the question is whether it's driving outcomes."

"I don't have time for another tool"

"That's exactly why we assign a dedicated success manager for 45-60 days. They set up everything, build campaigns, optimize. After that, automations run themselves."

"Can I start with just one product?"

"Absolutely. Most start with 1-2. That's the beauty of one system — the rest is there when you're ready. No new contract, no new vendor."

"My current vendor gives me an account manager"

"When's the last time they proactively shared a performance report? Suggested a campaign? Told you something about your business you didn't know? An account manager who responds to tickets is different from a revenue partner who drives growth."

"I need to see it working at a restaurant like mine"

This is a social proof ask — they want to know restaurants like theirs are succeeding. Name 2-3 similar ones, share high-level results (keep specifics vague), offer to show public-facing pages.

"$215/month is a lot for me right now"

"Every dollar matters. But rent and manpower are fixed whether you serve 50 or 100 customers. Every additional customer is almost pure margin. If the system drives 2-3 extra orders a month — very conservative — it's paid for itself. And you're building a database that compounds."

Value Tips Library

Drop these throughout the call to build trust and demonstrate F&B expertise. Each one helps the merchant even if they don't sign up.

Google search ranking

"When someone Googles your name, what shows up? If there's no direct ordering link, you're losing orders to marketplaces who rank for your name."

Google reviews > Instagram followers

"A customer deciding where to eat tonight is Googling, not scrolling Instagram. 100 reviews at 4.5 stars does more for walk-in traffic than 10K followers."

How to grow Google reviews

"Print a QR code linking to your Google review page. Put it on the table or hand it with the bill. Better yet — your Enrolment thank-you screen can include a review link."

Delivery fee psychology

"Customers are more sensitive to delivery fees than food markup. $21 delivery fee kills conversion. Mark up food 15% and charge $7 delivery? Same economics, way more orders."

The corporate order opportunity

"Corporates don't use GrabFood for team lunches. They Google '[cuisine] catering near me' or go to a restaurant they know. No ordering page? That $150 order goes to someone else."

Email frequency — the Nike lesson

"Most restaurants send one email a month. Nike sends two a week. Open rate is under 15% — but when you want sneakers, Nike is the first name in your head. Top-of-mind recall > open rates. Send weekly."

The 2nd visit is everything

"85% of first-timers don't return within a year. But get them to a 2nd visit — 30-40% chance of a 3rd. All energy should go to driving that 2nd visit."

Off-peak revenue hack

"Wednesday lunch is quiet? Send nearby customers a midweek offer Monday morning. 1,000 emails → 300 opens → 50 claims → 30 show up. 30 extra covers on your slowest day, for the cost of 30 drinks."

Weekend revenue for weekday shops

"Your kitchen is prepping weekends anyway. Party platters or corporate bundles via online ordering. 1-2 orders a weekend at $200-500 each = $2,000-4,000/month you don't get today."

Fixed cost leverage

"Manpower and rent are fixed whether you serve 50 or 100. Every additional customer is almost pure margin. That's why data capture and repeat visits are the highest-ROI activity in your business."
