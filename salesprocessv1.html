Module 4 · Equipping · Framework-first

Buyer Journey & Sales Process Design

How a restaurant owner moves from unaware of Oddle to signed and activated — and the exact role the rep plays at each step. Master this and every other module snaps into place: prospecting, pitching, closing, handoff all become moves along a single spine, not disconnected tactics.

~60 min read · 2× review Pre-reads: Modules 1 & 2 Outcome: rep can name every stage, exit criterion, and their job in each

Why this module exists

A rep who can't name the stages can't move deals. They work tactics — cold call, follow-up, demo — without knowing which tactic belongs to which moment. Deals stall and the rep can't tell where, because they don't have a map.

This module is the map. Five stages. Each with a buyer mindset, a rep job, a testable exit criterion, a common stall pattern, and a scripted move to unstick it. When the rep knows where a deal is, they know what to do next. When they don't, they guess.

Roberge's rule: the sales process is a mirror of the buyer journey. Stages have explicit exit criteria — not gut feel. If a rep moves a deal forward in Attio without meeting the exit criterion, the forecast lies and the coaching has nothing to fix.

Stage 1 of 5

Latent

Buyer hasn't named the problem · Rep must break attention

Merchant mental state

Inside the merchant's head "Another slow week. Marketplace is eating 30%. Hard to get new faces in. Whatever — back to the kitchen."

Busy operating. Problem felt, not named. Hasn't heard of Oddle, or has and shrugged. Skeptical of sales calls. Protective of time. Will not self-initiate.

Rep's role & activities

  • Own the outbound motion — cold call, targeted email, LinkedIn message, WhatsApp, in-person walk-in
  • Break attention with a restaurant-specific provocation — not a product pitch
  • Qualify the lead against ICP — is this a restaurant? is the price point in range? are they here for the long run?
  • Book the meeting — book it on the spot if possible, with a clear time and agenda

Marketing & GTM team contribute here

Marketing drives inbound through ads, content, peer referrals. GTM runs scaled tech outreach. Both feed the rep's Curious-stage pipeline. But the rep owns their own Latent-to-Curious conversion via cold prospecting — inbound is bonus, not the plan.

Testable exit criterion

Exit = Merchant confirms a specific calendar slot for a diagnostic pitch (date, time, location / video link). No slot, no advance.

Common stall pattern & unstick move

Stall: No reply across channels. Merchant not picking up. Unstick: switch channels (call → WhatsApp → walk-in), lead with a peer reference ("Chef K at [restaurant] uses us for X — 10 min to show you what they've seen?"), or pivot to a different decision-maker in the unit.

Scripted move — Stage 1 → 2

Rep opener "Hi [name], I'm [rep] from Oddle. I'm not pitching software — I run [role] and we help restaurants like [relevant peer] stop losing the 75% of customers who walk out without a name. Got 10 minutes Tuesday or Thursday for a quick diagnostic on your numbers?"
Stage 2 of 5

Curious

Meeting booked · Rep must diagnose and make them want more

Merchant mental state

Inside the merchant's head "Wait — so you're not a marketplace? What exactly do you do? Chef K uses you, right?"

Received a cold call, ad, or peer signal that broke through. Cautiously willing to give 15–30 minutes. Open to listen if the frame is right, will walk fast if pushed. Defensive posture — they've been pitched before.

Rep's role & activities

  • Run the 5-stage pitch — Discovery → Buy-In → Intro → Present → Close (see Module 6)
  • Diagnose the archetype — which of the 8 archetypes is this? (Module 2)
  • Let the merchant self-diagnose — Socratic questions, not product pitching
  • Quantify the leak — show the math on lost covers, missed frequency, unknown customers
  • End with a specific next step — not "we'll be in touch"

Testable exit criterion

Exit = Pitch completed and merchant verbally agrees there is a problem worth solving and agrees to receive a proposal. No verbal agreement, no advance — rep loops back or disqualifies.

Common stall pattern & unstick move

Stall: Meeting booked but rescheduled 2+ times, or merchant shows up distracted and non-committal. Unstick: send a 2-line pre-read 24 hours before to pre-commit their thinking ("I'll show you how [peer] grew repeat covers by X"); if they cancel again, park them in a 30-day nurture — don't burn cycles on a half-interested lead.

Scripted move — Stage 2 → 3 (end of pitch)

Rep close of pitch "Based on what you've told me — [quote their problem back: 'you said walk-ins are down 20% and you don't know who any of them are'] — it sounds like [Oddle product] would give you [quantified lever: +15% repeat cover rate]. Shall I put together a proposal and walk you through the numbers by [day]?"
Stage 3 of 5

Evaluating

Proposal out · Rep must prove & quantify · 3/7/14 cadence

Merchant mental state

Inside the merchant's head "Okay I see it. But will my team use it? What's the real cost? How is this different from Chope / Grab / the thing my POS guy sells? Show me someone like me who this worked for."

Problem named. Oddle is one of a few paths forward. Analytical mode. Asking detailed ROI, adoption, implementation, comparison questions. Weighing risk, not interest.

Rep's role & activities

  • Send the proposal within 1 day of the pitch — speed signals confidence
  • Run the 3/7/14 follow-up cadence — at days 3, 7, 14 post-proposal
  • Bring proof — peer case studies, ROI math, live demo, reference calls
  • Handle objections crisply — Module 6 objection library
  • Engage the full decision unit — if the first meeting was with a manager, get to the owner

Testable exit criterion

Exit = Proposal reviewed and merchant commits to a decision date and surfaces their remaining blockers. Advancing without a commitment date = false positive.

Common stall pattern & unstick move

Stall: Post-proposal ghost. Merchant opens the email but doesn't reply. Or meeting was with a manager, owner hasn't been looped in. Unstick: run the 3/7/14 cadence; at day 14, send a direct "what's blocking this?" ask with a yes/no bias. Offer an owner meeting. If the deal is real but the owner is travelling, that's a known pattern — keep it alive inside the 90-day window.
Reality check: Some deals legitimately take time. Owner travel, partner approval, end-of-month timing. Most deals sign within 60 days; remnants close in the final 30 of the 90-day window. Don't confuse a slow deal with a dead deal. Use the exit criterion — has the merchant committed to a decision date? Yes = alive. No = dead.

Scripted move — Stage 3 → 4 (post-proposal unstick)

Rep follow-up (day 7 or 14) "Hi [name], checking in on the proposal. If any part of it feels off, I'd rather talk it through than wait. What would make this a yes for you? If the answer is 'nothing right now,' say so and I'll come back at the right time."
Stage 4 of 5

Decision

Verbal yes · Rep must close paperwork and protect scope

Merchant mental state

Inside the merchant's head "Let me show this to my partner / accountant / head chef. Can we start after CNY? Send the proposal — but maybe hold the second product for now."

Mentally committed. Navigating final friction — price, timing, sign-off unit. Mild cold-feet risk. Needs reassurance, not more pitching. Often carves scope to feel safer.

Rep's role & activities

  • Close on value, not on discount — if price becomes the blocker, reframe the ROI math
  • Lock a specific start date — ambiguous timing kills momentum
  • Protect the scope — merchants try to carve down to one product at the last moment; hold the line on the full lever set, or explain what they lose
  • Drive paperwork to close — contract sent, signed, first invoice paid
  • Log everything in Attio — the handoff to CSM depends on this

Testable exit criterion

Exit = Contract signed and first invoice paid and kickoff meeting booked with CSM. A verbal yes is not a win — paid is the bar.

Common stall pattern & unstick move

Stall: Verbal yes but contract sitting unsigned for a week. Or merchant quietly stripping scope in the redlines. Unstick: bring forward a concrete start date with "let's lock X — I'll block the CSM calendar now"; for scope creep, send a 2-line "what we lose if we drop product Y" note.

Scripted move — Stage 4 → 5 (close)

Rep close "Let's lock it in — we can start on [specific date]. I'll send the paperwork now, and your CSM [name] will reach out within 48 hours to schedule kickoff. Any final questions before I hit send?"
Stage 5 of 5

Onboarded

Signed · CSM owns · Rep supports the handoff & LIR push

Merchant mental state

Inside the merchant's head "Did I make the right call? When does this actually start working? Why isn't the tablet picking up new customers yet?"

Signed. Anxious about the first 30 days. Wants fast proof. Vulnerable to buyer's remorse if activation drags. Every friction point is a churn risk.

Rep's role shifts — CSM owns the relationship here

  • Hand off cleanly to CSM — see handoff criteria below
  • Stay visible for the first 2 weeks — the merchant bought from you, not the CSM; disappearing breaks trust
  • Support the push to LIR — first 10 orders, first 500 enrolments, first email sent — the leading indicators that this will stick

Testable exit criterion (what "success" means at Stage 5)

Exit = Merchant hits LIR threshold within the first 30–60 days. See LIR thresholds below. This is the leading indicator that they will renew All Access Plan — and the real measure of whether the deal was "won."

Common stall pattern & unstick move

Stall: Low activation in first 30 days. Tablet not being used. Team not trained. Owner disengaged. Unstick: CSM-led but rep should re-engage the owner directly with a "how's it going?" — merchants open up to the person who sold them. Flag the churn risk to CSM early.

Scripted move — rep 2-week check-in

Rep check-in (week 2 post-sign) "Hi [name], just checking in as you hit your first week. I want to make sure [specific first milestone — first 10 orders / first 500 enrolments / first email sent] is happening. How's it looking?"

The funnel math — prospecting

Every rep must know their funnel by heart. You don't chase 20 pitches — you chase the top of the funnel, and the pitches fall out if the conversion rates hold.

April actuals (team · month-to-date)

Work the math backwards from the monthly target:

Target logic: Prospect 200 unique companies per month. At our 15% prospect-to-appointment conversion, that delivers 30 appointments — of which 20 become outbound pitches (some reschedule, some no-show, some spill into the next month). The rep's measured floor is 20 outbound pitches. Everything above is bonus.

Why 200 prospects is the right gauge

  • 200 prospects × 15% appointment rate = 30 appointments — a buffer above the 20-pitch floor
  • Buffer absorbs spillover — some April prospects become May pitches; that's normal
  • Prospecting is the one activity fully in the rep's control — inbound depends on Marketing, GTM tech outreach depends on the team, but cold prospecting is 100% you

The funnel math — pitch-to-win cohort waterfall

A pitch in January doesn't close in January. Most close in the 30 days after. Some close 30–60 days later. A few close 60–90 days later. This means a rep's calendar-month wins are the sum of cohorts that started 0, 30, and 60+ days ago.

Implication: A rep who crushes prospecting in their first month doesn't see the wins that month. They see them in month 2 and 3. This is why ramp exists — and why pipeline consistency matters more than any single month's activity.

Why the cohort waterfall matters — two rep takeaways

  1. Your Month 1 pain is not a skill problem — it's a cohort problem. You can only harvest 20% of your pitches in the same month. Trust the process.
  2. Consistency wins. A rep who pitches 20 every month hits 10 every month at steady state. A rep who pitches 30 one month and 10 the next will see feast-famine wins for the next 3 months. Pipeline flatness = revenue flatness.

Disqualification rules

Good reps disqualify fast. Bad reps hold onto dead deals and miss real ones. Every rep is expected to disqualify cleanly when these conditions are met:

Disqualify immediately if:
  • The lead is not a restaurant (retail, FMCG, grocery — not ICP)
  • The lead is not in our ICP price point (too low avg ticket to justify All Access Plan value)
  • The lead is a pop-up or short-run operation (closing within 6 months, seasonal pop-up, event-only)
Also disqualify (after following the cadence):
  • 90 days since first contact with no stage progression → auto-disqualify unless manager override
  • Merchant ghosted through the full 3/7/14 cadence twice
  • Verbal-agreed but contract unsigned > 30 days with no new signal

The 90-day deal cycle rule

Every Oddle deal has a 90-day life in the pipeline. After 90 days, it's auto-disqualified unless a manager override is applied.

Why 90 days?
  • Most deals close within 60 days of first contact
  • Remnants close in the final 30 days (days 60–90) — owner travel, partner approval, fiscal-year timing
  • Beyond 90 days the deal is statistically dead. Holding it in pipeline inflates the forecast and steals attention from live deals

Manager override — when 90 days is legitimately extended

  • Owner is travelling and has confirmed a specific return-to-decision date
  • Deal is tied to a known external event (new outlet opening, CNY, fiscal year)
  • Contract is signed but payment processing delayed through finance — in which case move to Decision → Onboarded, not extend Evaluating
Not a valid override: "merchant is still interested" / "just waiting" / "they said they'd get back to me." These are not commitments — they're stalls, and they are what the 90-day rule exists to clean out.

Common failure modes

Five patterns that kill deals and hide in reps' blind spots:

  1. Advancing the deal in Attio without meeting the exit criterion. Rep moves a deal to Evaluating because "the pitch went well" — but the merchant never verbally agreed to a problem. Forecast rots, coaching has nothing to fix.
  2. Confusing a slow deal with a dead deal. Rep drops a genuinely live 60-day deal because it's not closing fast enough. Or holds a ghost-lead past 90 days because "they said they'd come back." Use the exit criterion, not the feeling.
  3. Under-prospecting in Month 1. New hire focuses on the 4 pitches in front of them instead of building the pipeline for Month 2. Month 3 wins collapse.
  4. Disappearing after close. Rep signs the deal and ghosts. Merchant feels abandoned. Activation stalls. Handoff to CSM fails. Churn risk spikes.
  5. Scope-carving at the last moment. Merchant tries to drop a product at Decision stage. Rep accepts without showing what the merchant loses. LIR math now worse, renewal risk up.

Check for understanding

Before advancing to Module 5, a rep should be able to answer all of the below with no materials in front of them:

  1. Name the 5 Oddle buyer stages in order, and state the merchant's mental state in one sentence for each.
  2. For each stage, state the testable exit criterion — not a description, a criterion that can be checked yes/no.
  3. Who owns each stage? Where does Marketing contribute? Where does GTM contribute? Where does CSM take over?
  4. What is the math that says 200 prospected companies per month delivers ~30 appointments?
  5. Given 20 outbound pitches per month, how many wins should a fully-ramped rep expect per calendar month? Show the cohort waterfall.
  6. What is the 90-day rule? What are the three valid override reasons? What are two common invalid override excuses?
  7. What are the three disqualification criteria for immediate drop?
  8. What is LIR? What is the standalone threshold for Oddle Shop? For Reserve? For Enrolment?
  9. What is the combined LIR insight — why does the rep always position All Access Plan at close?
  10. Name two common failure modes a rep falls into, and the fix.
