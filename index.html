The Five Gaps

Every restaurant has some combination of these. Your job is to find which ones, quantify them, and help the merchant see them clearly.

GapWhat It MeansKey Question
Data GapMost of their customers are invisible. No contact info, no way to bring them back. Reservations only capture the booker — not the group."How many diners per month? How many do you actually have data on?"
Insight GapEven when they have data, they don't know WHY customers come — which channel, how far they travel, what drives return visits."Do you know how your customers discover you?"
Engagement GapThey're not looking at their data, not segmenting customers, and not engaging them to drive repeat visits. Data sits idle."What are you doing today to bring customers back? Are you segmenting and targeting, or sending the same message to everyone?"
Revenue GapThey're missing entire revenue channels — corporate orders, group gatherings, direct delivery — because they only work with marketplaces."Do you think customers order for families, offices, groups? Are you capturing those?"
Operational GapTheir current tools are fragmented, expensive, or underperforming. They lack visibility into traffic, conversion, and logistics."Do you know your online traffic? Conversion rate? Who handles logistics issues?"
The sequence is flexible. The thoroughness is not.
You don't always go Data → Insight → Engagement → Revenue → Operations. You lead with whatever the merchant cares about most. But by the end of the diagnostic, you should have covered all five. Think of them as a checklist, not a script.

Sales Philosophy

The mindset behind everything in this guide.

Diagnose, Don't Pitch

A doctor doesn't walk into the room and say "you need surgery." They ask questions, run tests, and help the patient understand what's happening. Then the patient asks for help. That's what we do.

You are not here to sell software. You are here to help a restaurant owner see — with their own numbers and their own words — where they're leaking revenue, overpaying, or flying blind. The pitch becomes inevitable because the merchant already sees the problem.

Questions Drive Insight, Not Statements

When you tell a merchant "you have a data gap," they can disagree. When you ask "how many of your 5,000 diners do you actually have contact info for?" and they calculate 500 — they can't disagree with their own math. The question is always more powerful than the statement.

Guide Thinking, Don't Accept "I Don't Know"

"I don't know" is not a dead end. It's one of three things:

What they meanHow to read itWhat you do
"I'm not the right person to answer"They genuinely don't manage that area.Use ballpark numbers. "Let's estimate — roughly how many diners would you say on a busy weekend?"
"I've never checked"The data exists but they haven't looked. This IS the gap.Normalise it: "That's very common — you're busy running the restaurant." Then offer: "Would it help if we pulled it up together right now?"
"I know but I haven't acted on it"They know the numbers but haven't done anything about it.This is where you go deeper: "What's stopped you from acting on it?"

Quantification Creates Urgency

Saying "you have a data gap" is abstract. Saying "out of 5,000 diners, you only know 500 — the other 4,500 walk out and you have no way to bring them back" is concrete. Always do the math with the merchant. Let them arrive at the number.

Let the Merchant Arrive at the Conclusion

Never say "you need Oddle." Instead, surface the gaps, quantify them, summarize them, and ask: "Would you like to see how other restaurants have solved this?" When they say yes — that's the sale.

The No-Pitch Rule
For the first 10–15 minutes of any meeting: no pitching. Only questions. If you're talking about Oddle's features before you've diagnosed their gaps, you've jumped ahead. Go back.

1 min Step 1: Rapport

Warm the conversation. Transition quickly.

"How's business?"

That's it. Light rapport — food, familiarity if you've been to the restaurant, a genuine compliment if something stood out. Do not overextend. You're not here to be their friend yet. You're here to earn 30 minutes of their attention.

Common mistake
New reps spend 5–10 minutes on rapport because they're nervous about starting the diagnostic. One minute is enough. The best rapport is built through a great diagnostic conversation, not small talk.

Understand why they took the meeting. This calibrates everything that follows.

"What made you want to meet us?"

Their answer tells you where to start the diagnostic. It doesn't change what gaps you cover — it changes the order.

No matter where you start, by the time you get to the Summary (Step 5), you should have explored all five gaps (Data, Insight, Engagement, Revenue, Operations). The merchant's intent tells you the entry point, not the destination.

Step 3: Set the Diagnostic Frame

Ask permission. Position yourself as a consultant, not a salesperson.

"Before I share more about what we do, I'd like to understand your business first — where the gaps are, where the opportunities might be. Would it be okay if I ask you a few questions?"

This does three things:

What it doesWhy it matters
Establishes controlYou're leading the conversation, not reacting to their questions.
Positions you as a consultantYou're here to understand their business, not demo software.

What if they say "just show me what you have"?

How many bookings per month?" You're still diagnosing, just inside the presentation. See Must-Have Data Points for what to capture even in a rushed meeting.

Step 4: The Diagnostic Engine

This is the core of the entire system. Find the gaps. Quantify them. Let the merchant see them.

1 Data Gap — Customer Identification

The Anchor Question

"On a monthly basis, roughly how many diners do you serve?"

This is the denominator for everything. Every gap calculation starts here.

If they don't know their diner count

Don't let it go. Guide them to an estimate using one of these methods:

MethodHow it works
Revenue-based"What's your monthly revenue, roughly?" → "What's your average spend per customer?" → Divide to get diners.
Daily flow"How many diners on a typical weekday? And weekend?" → Multiply out to monthly.
The fact that they don't know is itself a data point
"That's interesting — most restaurants can estimate from their POS. Do you track covers per day?" If they can't answer the most basic question about their business, the diagnostic has already begun.

Reservation Breakdown

#QuestionWhat you're listening forNext move
2"Are you using reservations? How many bookings per month?"The known portion. Usually 30-50% of diners for full-service.Get the number. Make sure they're clear on bookings vs diners (common mistake — see below).
3"How many covers per booking, on average?"Usually 2–4. This lets you calculate individuals vs bookings.Now do the math together — out loud.
Watch out: Bookings ≠ Diners, and Known ≠ Total reservation diners

Mistake 1: Merchants confuse bookings with diners. If they say "5,000 bookings = 5,000 diners," clarify: "So 5,000 bookings at 3 per group — that's 15,000 diners, not 5,000."

Mistake 2 (even reps make this one): Don't say "500 bookings × 3 = 1,500 known customers." The reservation only captures the booker. The other 2 people at the table gave you nothing — no name, no phone, no email. Known customers from 500 reservations = 500, not 1,500.

The Gap Reveal

Critical: Reservations only capture the BOOKER
500 bookings × 3 per group = 1,500 diners who came through reservations. But the reservation only captures the one person who made the booking. The other 2 people at the table? Completely invisible. So from reservations, you know 500 customers, not 1,500.
Do the math together — out loud
"So roughly 5,000 diners a month, 500 reservations at 3 per group — that's 1,500 diners who came through bookings. But the reservation only captures the person who booked — not the other 2 people at the table. So you actually know 500 customers out of 5,000. That's 90% of your customers walking out the door and you have no idea who they are."

The merchant doing this math is more powerful than any slide you could show them. 90% invisible hits much harder than 70%. Let the silence work.

Loyalty / Data Capture Check

If they have a loyalty program, CRM, or members system:

#QuestionWhat you're listening forNext move
4"How many new members does it add per month?"Almost always less than 20% of total covers.Calculate against total diners: "So 200 members, but 5,000 diners. That's 4%."
5"How many returning members per month?"The active engagement rate. Usually low.Now you have: known customers vs unknown customers. The gap is quantified.
2 Insight Gap — Customer Understanding

The Data Gap shows they don't know WHO their customers are. The Insight Gap shows they don't know WHY they come.

#QuestionWhat you're listening forNext move
6"Do you know how your customers discover you? TikTok? Instagram? Walk-ins? Word of mouth?"Almost no merchant can answer this accurately.If they guess: "How do you know? Is that measured, or a gut feel?"
7"Do you know if your customers live nearby or travel here?"No one knows this."Because the lunch crowd, the weekday regulars — those are people nearby. If you knew who they were, you could fill slow periods with one targeted message."
The insight gap summary
"So you don't just have a data gap — you also have an understanding gap. You don't know who your customers are, and for the ones you do know, you don't know why they came, how they found you, or whether they're nearby."
3 Engagement Gap — Not Using Data to Drive Repeat

This is the gap between having customer data and actually doing something with it. Many restaurants capture some data — through reservations, loyalty, or even a spreadsheet — but they're not segmenting, not targeting, and not engaging customers to drive repeat visits.

#QuestionIf they say...Your response
8"What are you doing today to bring customers back?""Nothing really""So a customer dines with you, enjoys it, and then... nothing. No message, no offer, no reminder. Whether they come back is entirely up to chance?"
"We post on Instagram / social media""That reaches followers — but does it reach the specific customer who dined with you last Tuesday and hasn't been back? Social media is broadcast. Engagement is personal."
"We send emails / promotions"Good — go to Q9.
9"When you send, do you send the same message to everyone, or do you segment?""Same to everyone""So your VIP who comes weekly and a first-timer who came once three months ago get the same message? Would you talk to those two customers the same way in person?"
"We try to segment""What segments? How are you deciding who gets what?" Usually they'll reveal it's very basic — if at all.
10"Do you know your return visit rate? Of all the customers who dine with you, what percentage come back within 60 or 90 days?"Almost never known."Industry average: about 85% of diners don't return within a year. But here's the thing — if you can get them to a 2nd visit, there's a 30-40% chance of a 3rd. The 2nd visit is the most valuable moment in your business. Are you doing anything to drive it?"
The engagement gap summary
"So you have some customer data, but you're not really using it to drive behaviour. You're not segmenting, you're not targeting based on visit frequency or proximity, and you don't know your return rate. The data is sitting there — it's just not working for you yet."
4 Revenue Gap — Missing Sales Channels

This applies mainly to merchants using marketplaces for delivery/takeaway.

#QuestionIf they say...Your response
8"For your delivery orders, most orders are below $50, right?""Yes, around $20-30""Those are impulse orders — one person, last minute, hungry now. That's what marketplaces are great at."
9"Do you think customers also order for families, offices, group gatherings?""Yes, sometimes" or "I think so""Those orders are $100-120 on average. They're planned 6+ hours in advance. Completely different customer. Are you capturing those today?"
10"Why do you think you're not capturing these orders?"Usually: no channel for it / haven't thought about it"Marketplaces serve impulse. They're not designed for corporate or group orders. That's an entirely different sales channel you don't have yet."
Critical: eShop is a new sales channel, not a cheaper marketplace
Never position Oddle eShop as "save on marketplace commissions." Marketplace customers and direct customers are fundamentally different people making fundamentally different orders. It's Nike and Footlocker — Nike doesn't tell Footlocker to stop selling shoes. Nike opens its own store for a different kind of customer experience.
5 Operational Gap — Systems & Efficiency

For merchants who have their own systems (own website ordering, standalone reservation tool, etc.):

#QuestionWhat you're listening forWhat it reveals
11"Do you know your online traffic? How many people visit your ordering page?"Almost never tracked.They can't improve what they can't measure.
12"What's your conversion rate?"Blank stare or "I don't know."They don't know the formula: Traffic × Conversion × ABS = Revenue.
13"Who handles logistics issues? Late deliveries, wrong orders, customer complaints?"Usually the merchant themselves or a staff member.Operational drag. Time spent on logistics is time not spent on the restaurant.
The eShop revenue formula
Most restaurants say "I want to increase online sales" but don't know which lever to pull. The formula is Traffic × Conversion × Average Basket Size = Revenue. When you help a merchant see this formula for the first time, they suddenly understand where the gap is — low traffic? low conversion? low basket size? — and what to focus on.

Cost savings is a strong lever. In the summary: "You're paying $X per month for tools that don't talk to each other. Oddle is $215 for everything — unified." Lead with cost in the summary alongside growth.

Don't compete on price — you'll lose. Focus entirely on value growth: "Your current setup costs less, but is it helping you grow? Can it capture dine-in data? Can it drive repeat visits? Can it open a new sales channel?" The question isn't what it costs — it's what it's costing you in missed opportunity.

Write down the number
The total monthly spend is powerful in the summary. "You're paying $950 a month across 4 different tools, and none of them share data. Oddle gives you all of that plus data capture and engagement for $215." But only use it when the number works in your favour.
⚠ Siloed Data — The Hidden Problem

This comes up when a merchant already has multiple organic tools — their own reservation system, their own eShop, maybe a loyalty program. They think they're covered. But ask:

#QuestionIf they say...Your response
—"These tools you're using — are they connected? Does your reservation data feed into your marketing? Does your eShop know who your dine-in customers are?""No, they're separate" (almost always)"So a customer books a table on Saturday, orders delivery on Tuesday, and signs up for your loyalty on Wednesday — and you see that as three separate people?"
—"How are you analysing the data across these systems? Are you pulling reports from each one separately?""Yes" / "We don't really analyse""How much time does that take? And even when you do — can you answer a simple question like 'how many of my reservation customers also order delivery'?"
—"Are you spending time manually exporting, combining spreadsheets, trying to get a complete picture?""Yes, it's a lot of work""What if all of that was automatic? One customer profile, every touchpoint — dine-in, delivery, reservation — in one view. No exporting, no spreadsheets."
Why this matters
Siloed data is the reason merchants can't do effective engagement even when they have tools. They can't segment because the data is in 4 different places. They can't track the customer journey because each system only sees one touchpoint. This is the strongest argument for a unified system — and it's the hardest thing for competitors to replicate. One customer, one profile, every channel.

"Are you at maximum capacity?"
This question helps you find the areas of growth. Ask it when exploring where the merchant can improve.

Step 5: The Summary

Mirror back what the merchant told you. In their language, with their numbers.

This is where the diagnostic turns into a mandate. You're not telling them they have problems — you're reflecting their own words back to them.

The Three Moves

1 Acknowledge
"Thank you for sharing all of that. Everything you've told me is really important and helpful in terms of how I see your potential cost leakage, revenue leakage, and growth opportunities."

You're telling the merchant: what you just told me wasn't small talk. It was valuable. That earns trust.

2 Stack the Gaps — Lead with Revenue

Always start with revenue growth. That's what every merchant cares about most. Then layer on the other gaps.

"From the looks of it, it seems like you have great potential to grow in terms of your takeaway and delivery — especially those corporate and group orders we talked about. And for dine-in, there's a whole opportunity to drive more return visits.

But what I also see is a data gap. From the [X] diners you do per month and the [Y] bookings you get, there's a large portion of customers you have no data on. And based on what you shared, you're also not sure where your customers are coming from or whether they're nearby.

So you want to increase sales — and the data and insights are what will let you do that intelligently."
Use their numbers, not yours
Always reference the specific numbers they gave you during the diagnostic. "From the 5,000 diners and 500 bookings" — not "most restaurants have a data gap." Their numbers make it personal and undeniable.
3 The Invitation
"This is where Oddle can come in to help. Are you keen to see how you can grow sales, potentially lower cost, and also get all these insights and act on them?"

One question. Three value propositions: grow sales, lower cost, gain insights. You're asking permission again before moving into the product introduction. When they say yes — you've earned the right to present.

Adapt to what matters most
If the merchant spent the whole diagnostic talking about cost (paying too much for reservations, high marketplace commissions), lead the summary with cost: "You're currently paying $X for tool A and $Y for tool B — $Z total for just two functions. Based on everything you've shared, I think Oddle can help you lower cost while also empowering you with the data capture and engagement tools you're missing."

Objection Handling

Objections that come up during or after the pitch.

"Your eShop is more expensive than what I have"

This comes up when the merchant has an existing eShop with a flat subscription fee and no usage-based pricing. Oddle charges 6.5% on GMV above $3,000/month, so their mental math says "Oddle costs more."

Response: Two angles

Angle 1 — Surface their current gaps first

QuestionWhat it exposes
"Can your eShop sales be higher?"If yes — they need growth tools, not just a checkout page.
"Are you having logistics issues?"If yes — Oddle handles logistics end-to-end. Their current tool doesn't.
"Do you know how to grow online sales if you wanted to?"If no — they're missing the Traffic × Conversion × ABS framework.
"Is your eShop data unified with your dine-in and reservation data?"Almost always no. Their eShop is a silo.

Angle 2 — Justify the cost with growth math

"Let's say your current eShop does $10,000/month. Logistics and payment fees are similar on both sides — so the real difference is the 6.5% above $3,000, which is about $455.

But what if we drive $1,000 or $2,000 more in sales through abandoned cart recovery, corporate orders, and the Oddle Eats marketing channel? You've more than recouped the difference. And that's before counting the dine-in customers we can drive back through email.

So the question is: do you believe we can drive more sales? And if we can — why are so many merchants choosing Oddle over a cheaper alternative? It's about the value of the system, not the cost of one tool."

Angle 3 — Reframe the comparison

"Don't compare eShop to eShop. Compare your total tech stack to Oddle. You're paying $X for reservations, $Y for your eShop, and you still don't have data capture, CDP, or email marketing. Oddle gives you everything for $215/month."
Exception: single-product merchants
If a merchant truly only needs one product (e.g., a fine dining restaurant that only wants reservations), you can't use the total-stack comparison. In this case, compete on the product merits and highlight that everything else is available whenever they're ready. "You're getting reservations now, but the moment you want to capture dine-in data or run email campaigns — it's already there. No new contract, no new vendor."

Why prioritisation matters
A merchant who already has 30,000 customer records and limited time doesn't need to hear about data capture first. They need to hear about what they can DO with that data, or about the revenue channel they're missing. Always lead with the biggest impact or opportunity — not a fixed sequence.
